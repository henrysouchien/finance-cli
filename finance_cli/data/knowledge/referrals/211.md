# 211 (United Way Community-Services Hotline)

211 is the national three-digit dialing code reserved by the FCC for community-services information and referral. United Way Worldwide operates the program at the national level, but actual call handling is hyper-local: a 211 call connects to a state or local 211 affiliate organization with trained specialists who know the programs operating in that specific community. The service is free, confidential, and operates 24/7 in most service areas. Most 211 callers are routed to programs for food assistance, housing and rental help, utility assistance, healthcare access, mental health and crisis support, employment support, and disaster recovery.

The critical distinction for coaching: **211 is primarily a navigator/referral surface, not a uniform application surface**. A 211 specialist helps a client identify *which* programs they may be eligible for and *who actually administers* them locally. Some local 211 affiliates additionally offer application or intake assistance for specific programs — for example, certain affiliates can complete and submit SNAP applications by phone — but that capability varies by affiliate. In all cases, the eligibility determination and enrollment authority remain with the administering program: SNAP eligibility is decided by the state SNAP office, LIHEAP by a state-designated administrator, rental assistance by the local public housing agency or community-action agency. 211 closes the awareness gap and sometimes the intake-friction gap; it never substitutes for the administering program's eligibility decision.

Coverage is broad but not uniform. Most U.S. zip codes have functioning 211 service through a state or regional affiliate; some rural areas have thinner coverage or rely on 211 offerings from a neighboring region. When 211 doesn't reach the client's location, the coach can route to the state Department of Health and Human Services 2-1-1-equivalent line or the state community-action agency directory directly.

## Use When

Surface 211 as a referral when:

- The client mentions a **specific unmet basic need** — running short on food, behind on utilities at shutoff threshold, facing eviction, needing emergency rental assistance, struggling to afford prescriptions — but doesn't know which program covers that need or which local agency administers it.
- The client is **eligible for or already receiving** benefits like SNAP, TANF, Medicaid, WIC, Section 8, or LIHEAP and asks about adjacent programs that pair with their existing enrollment (energy assistance, food banks, free or reduced-cost healthcare clinics).
- The client is in **immediate crisis** with a community-services dimension (housing instability, food insecurity, mental health crisis, domestic violence safety planning, disaster recovery) and needs both immediate help and a routing path to longer-term services.
- The client is **navigating a system change** that exposes them to new community-services eligibility (job loss, new disability, recent divorce, military separation, recent move to a new state, recent immigration status change) and would benefit from a local specialist who knows what's actually available in the new context.
- The coach is **scope-limited** on a specific program question — 211 specialists know the state-specific and locally-administered details (which utility provider participates in LIHEAP, which food pantries operate this week, which legal-aid clinic handles a specific issue type) that a general coach should not pretend to know.

Distinguish 211 from program-specific routes: 211 is the right surface when the client knows the *need* but not the *program*. If the client already knows the program (e.g., applying for SNAP), the route is the state SNAP office or a locally authorized application-assistance partner — which may include certain 211 affiliates that have application-help capability. Distinguish from NFCC/FCAA: NFCC and FCAA route to nonprofit credit counseling specifically; 211 routes across the much broader community-services landscape (food, housing, utilities, healthcare, mental health, etc.).
