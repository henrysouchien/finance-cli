# RMD And Distribution Basics

Required minimum distribution readiness helps the user recognize when
retirement-account withdrawals become mandatory and which facts a CPA or
fiduciary needs. It is not a tax filing conclusion, withdrawal schedule, or
account-by-account instruction.

Use this topic when the user asks about RMDs, IRA or workplace-plan
distributions, withholding, taxable income, inherited accounts, or whether they
can ignore an account for retirement-income planning.

## Key Concepts

**RMD rules depend on account type and owner status.** IRS RMD rules apply to
traditional IRAs, SEP IRAs, SIMPLE IRAs, 401(k), 403(b), 457(b), profit-sharing
and other defined-contribution plans, and Roth IRA beneficiaries. Roth IRA
owners generally have different rules than beneficiaries.

**The first RMD year is age-based and law-sensitive.** Current IRS materials
state that covered retirement funds generally must begin distributions when the
owner reaches age 73. Do not quote or calculate RMD ages, deadlines, or
exceptions from memory in a user answer; use current IRS source metadata or a
deterministic helper.

**Calculation needs prior-year balance and a distribution period.** IRS
describes the basic RMD calculation as the prior year-end account balance
divided by a distribution period from the appropriate IRS table, with different
rules in some spouse-beneficiary situations.

**Distribution taxation is not the same as distribution mechanics.** IRS
Publication 590-B covers IRA distributions, and IRS Publication 575 covers
pension and annuity income. Taxable amount, basis, withholding, penalties,
rollovers, and reporting can require CPA review.

**More than the minimum may be withdrawn.** IRS notes that a person can withdraw
more than the required minimum, but taking more is a planning and tax question,
not a default recommendation.

## Data To Gather

Capture:

- birth year and whether the account owner is living or deceased;
- account type and owner;
- prior December 31 balance if an RMD calculation is being reviewed;
- beneficiary and spouse-age facts when relevant;
- whether the account is inherited;
- whether the owner is still working and the plan type, if a workplace plan is
  involved;
- prior distributions already taken for the year;
- tax withholding, estimated-tax, and filing-status context;
- source metadata for any current-year table, age, or rule used.

## CashNerd Use

Allowed output:

- explain that RMDs are mandatory minimum withdrawals for covered accounts;
- identify whether RMD relevance is unknown, future, current, or
  professional-review-needed;
- list facts needed for a CPA or fiduciary;
- surface IRS source names and retrieval dates;
- preserve the user's RMD question for a handoff packet.

Not allowed:

- represent an RMD calculation as final tax advice;
- file or prepare a tax return position;
- choose withholding or estimated-tax amounts;
- tell the user which account to withdraw from first;
- advise rollover, conversion, QCD, or beneficiary strategy as an
  implementation decision.

## Handoff Triggers

Route to a CPA or fiduciary when:

- the user is at or near RMD age;
- an inherited account is involved;
- prior-year balances, beneficiary status, spouse age, or prior distributions
  are incomplete;
- the user wants tax withholding, QCD, Roth conversion, rollover, or withdrawal
  order advice;
- a plan document, annuity contract, divorce order, trust, estate, or
  beneficiary legal question controls the answer.

## Common Pitfalls

- Treating all retirement accounts as having identical RMD rules.
- Forgetting inherited-account differences.
- Quoting an RMD age or divisor without current IRS metadata.
- Ignoring tax withholding and estimated tax.
- Treating the minimum distribution as the recommended spending amount.
- Combining accounts or plan balances incorrectly.
- Letting a retirement-income plan proceed without identifying RMD relevance.
