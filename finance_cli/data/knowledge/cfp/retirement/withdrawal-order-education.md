# Withdrawal Order Education

Withdrawal-order education helps the user understand why retirement withdrawals
need coordinated tax, investment, cash-flow, RMD, and risk review. It does not
tell the user which account to withdraw from first.

Use this topic when a user asks how to spend down accounts, whether to use
taxable money before retirement accounts, how RMDs interact with withdrawals,
or whether Roth conversions, annuities, or pension income change the sequence.

## Key Concepts

**Withdrawal order is an implementation decision.** Account type, tax basis,
ordinary income, capital gains, RMDs, penalties, healthcare subsidies, Medicare
premiums, estate goals, risk tolerance, and market conditions can all matter.
The coach should not produce a one-size-fits-all order.

**Account location and tax treatment matter.** Taxable brokerage, traditional
retirement, Roth, HSA, pension, annuity, cash, and business accounts can have
different tax and liquidity effects. The user needs current balances, basis,
distribution rules, and professional review.

**RMDs can override preferences.** If RMDs are current or near, the minimum
required distribution and tax treatment may constrain the plan. RMD readiness
must be checked before a withdrawal sequence is treated as usable.

**Investments and sequence risk matter.** Selling assets to fund spending can
change portfolio risk, concentration, taxes, and longevity. The coach may
explain the need for review, not choose securities or rebalance the portfolio.

**Cash-flow timing matters.** Retirement spending, emergency reserves, bridge
income, Medicare timing, taxes, and large known expenses determine how much
cash is needed and when.

## Data To Gather

Capture:

- current and target retirement spending;
- essential versus discretionary spending;
- emergency reserves and near-term large expenses;
- account balances by tax type;
- cost basis and unrealized gains for taxable accounts, if relevant;
- RMD relevance and distribution history;
- pension, annuity, and Social Security expected timing;
- tax bracket, filing status, withholding, and CPA involvement;
- investment allocation and risk tolerance status;
- estate, beneficiary, charitable, or healthcare subsidy concerns.

## CashNerd Use

Allowed output:

- explain why withdrawal sequencing is complex;
- identify missing data;
- prepare a withdrawal-order review checklist;
- mark implementation as professional-review-needed;
- suggest that the user ask a fiduciary and CPA to compare sequences.

Not allowed:

- produce a personalized account withdrawal order;
- recommend selling securities, funds, or asset classes;
- recommend Roth conversions, QCDs, tax-loss harvesting, or tax-bracket
  management as an implementation plan;
- choose withholding or estimated-tax amounts;
- represent a rough gap model as a withdrawal strategy.

## Handoff Questions

Useful questions for a fiduciary planner or CPA:

- Which accounts should fund the first year of retirement spending, and why?
- How do RMDs, Social Security, pension income, and annuities change the order?
- What tax brackets, Medicare premiums, or subsidy cliffs should be modeled?
- Which taxable lots or account types would create tax consequences?
- How should emergency reserves and market downturns be handled?
- Are Roth conversions, QCDs, or charitable strategies worth reviewing?

## Common Pitfalls

- Assuming "taxable first, traditional second, Roth last" always applies.
- Ignoring RMDs and withholding.
- Treating an investment rebalance as a withdrawal plan.
- Using average annual spending without near-term cash timing.
- Forgetting healthcare, Medicare, and insurance premiums.
- Letting the AI choose a sequence when professional review is required.
