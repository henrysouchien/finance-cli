# Building Credit

Credit strategy is stage-dependent. A young adult with no credit file, a homeowner with a 780 score, and a client rebuilding after a bankruptcy discharge have almost nothing in common operationally. The Build / Maintain / Rebuild frame from AFCPE forces the counselor to diagnose the stage before prescribing actions. Advice that is correct for one stage is usually wrong for another.

The underlying mechanics — what a report is, how the score is computed, how utilization is calculated — live in the credit-reports-and-scores topic. This topic assumes those are already understood and focuses on the stage-based strategy plus the honest conversation about credit-repair companies.

## Diagnose the Stage First

Three stages, with different goals:

- **Build** — thin file or no file. Young adult, recent immigrant, someone whose financial life has been cash-only, or a spouse whose accounts were all in the other partner's name. The problem is the absence of history.
- **Maintain** — established, healthy credit. Goal is to protect what already exists, avoid unforced errors, and not damage the score ahead of a planned major application.
- **Rebuild** — negative items on the file, a depressed score, or both. After a job loss, medical crisis, divorce, bankruptcy, or period of mismanagement. The problem is specific historical damage plus the absence of new positive data to counterweight it.

A single client may sit in different stages across different dimensions (long history of on-time payments, but recent high utilization edging toward Rebuild). Pick the dominant story; treat edge cases as sub-points.

## Stage 1: Build

**Goal: establish a credit file and create initial positive payment history.**

A credit file needs at least one tradeline — an open account with a creditor reporting to the bureaus — to exist at all. Scoring models generally require ~6 months of reporting data before producing a meaningful score. Build tactics aim at getting a first reporting account without requiring credit history the client doesn't yet have.

Core moves:

- **Secured credit card.** Client deposits collateral (typically $200–$500); receives a card with a credit limit roughly equal to the deposit. Functions like a normal credit card; reports to bureaus; carries standard terms. After consistent on-time payments, the issuer typically returns the deposit and converts the card to unsecured, or the client graduates elsewhere. Most common and reliable Build path.
- **Credit-builder loan.** A small installment loan ($500–$2,000) where the borrowed amount sits in a savings account held by the lender; the borrower makes monthly payments and receives the funds at the end of the term. Payments report to bureaus, creating installment-loan history. Offered by many credit unions and community development lenders.
- **Authorized user on another account.** Client is added to a primary cardholder's existing card and inherits that card's history (positive and negative) onto their own report. Effective when the primary holder has a long, clean history; damaging if the primary's history is poor. Relationship-dependent.
- **Alternative data reporting.** Services that report on-time rent, utility, and streaming-subscription payments to the bureaus (Experian Boost, various rent-reporting services). Coverage is bureau-specific and effect on score varies by product.
- **Starter-tier cards from the client's existing bank or credit union.** Relationship accounts are easier to open than standalone applications.

What to avoid in Build:

- **Multiple simultaneous applications.** Each application is a hard inquiry; the new-credit component penalizes clusters. Open one starter account, let it season for ~6 months, evaluate.
- **"Credit-building" products with hidden fees.** Some store cards and "credit repair" cards layer on annual, application, and program fees that turn a $200 credit line into a $150 credit line at 29% APR. A plain secured card from a mainstream issuer is almost always cheaper.
- **Co-signing a loan for someone else.** The client becomes legally liable for the whole balance; downside is concentrated and real. Available installment history through cheaper, safer channels.

## Stage 2: Maintain

**Goal: protect what's already built and avoid unforced errors.**

For a client with solid credit, the entire game is not damaging the score. Maintain moves are defensive:

- **Pay on time, always, on every account.** Payment history is 35% of the score; a single 30-day-late payment can drop a good score by 50–100 points and remains on the report for 7 years per FCRA. Autopay for the minimum payment on every account is a single configuration that eliminates the single biggest risk. The client can still pay more (ideally the full statement balance) manually; autopay is the backstop.
- **Keep utilization low.** Stay below 30% on any individual card and in aggregate; under 10% is better. Strategies for spending spikes: mid-cycle payment before the statement cuts, request a limit increase, or split spending across cards.
- **Preserve old accounts.** The oldest account anchors the length-of-history component. Closing the oldest card or loan can dent the score years later when the closed account ages off. Keep them open; make a small charge every few months to prevent issuer-initiated closure.
- **Limit new credit applications.** Each application is a hard inquiry; clusters read as financial stress. Space applications at least 6 months apart; consolidate when rate-shopping (most scoring models treat multiple mortgage or auto-loan inquiries within a short window as a single inquiry).
- **Monitor regularly.** Quarterly report pulls through annualcreditreport.com, plus score tracking through any free-access product, catch problems early.
- **Review before major applications.** 6–12 months before a mortgage, auto loan, or other large application, pull all three reports, dispute errors, pay down utilization, and avoid new accounts.

What to avoid in Maintain:

- **Closing unused cards thinking it's "cleaning up."** Usually damages the score — lower total limits raise utilization, and the closed account eventually ages off.
- **Applying for store cards at checkout for a discount.** The 20% off is rarely worth the hard inquiry, new-account penalty, and increased risk of carrying a balance on a 25%+ APR card.
- **Carrying a balance "to build credit."** Persistent myth. Paying in full each month is the optimal move; there is no benefit to carrying a balance.

## Stage 3: Rebuild

**Goal: overcome negative history by adding new positive data and, where possible, removing legitimately disputable negative data.**

Rebuild is harder and slower than Build. Negative items (late payments, collections, charge-offs, bankruptcies) stay on a report for up to 7 years from the date of the original delinquency per FCRA; 10 years for Chapter 7 bankruptcy. The strategy is two-track: work the negatives, and simultaneously establish new positive tradelines.

Negatives track:

- **Pull all three reports.** Rebuild starts with knowing exactly what's on the file, bureau by bureau.
- **Dispute errors.** Roughly a quarter of reports have at least one error. Dispute inaccurate items with the bureau and, in parallel, with the creditor. Documentation (payment confirmations, settlement letters) materially improves success rates.
- **Address collections strategically.** A paid collection is generally better than an unpaid one, but the account still appears as a derogatory item. Some newer scoring models (FICO 9+, VantageScore 3+) ignore paid collections; many lenders still use older models that don't. For collections the client intends to pay, consider asking for a **pay-for-delete** agreement — the collector agrees in writing to remove the item in exchange for payment. Not all collectors will agree.
- **Do not restart the clock on old debt.** A collection on a debt that has aged past the statute of limitations (varies by state; typically 3–6 years for most debts) is usually not legally enforceable through lawsuit, though it may still appear on the report. Making a payment or even acknowledging the debt in writing can restart the SOL clock in some states. Consult debt-default-and-collections before paying a collector on a stale debt.

Positives track:

- **Secured products.** A secured credit card post-Rebuild works the same way as in Build — establishes new on-time payment data. The difference is context: in Build it's the first tradeline; in Rebuild it counterweights existing negative tradelines.
- **Relentless on-time payments on every active account.** Monthly new positive data points. A 12-month streak of on-time payments across all open accounts materially reshapes the score even before negatives age off.
- **Strict utilization management.** Under 10% if possible. Utilization swings are one of the fastest ways to move the score; controlling it aggressively during Rebuild pays off quickly.
- **Prioritize delinquent accounts.** Any account still showing late status is actively damaging the score month by month. Bringing it current stops the ongoing damage.
- **Specialized "second chance" products.** Some credit unions and community lenders offer products designed for post-bankruptcy or post-collection rebuilding. Pricing is higher than mainstream but typically much lower than predatory alternatives.

Pacing expectation: a client rebuilding from a severe event (bankruptcy, multiple charge-offs, long missed-payment period) is typically looking at **18–36 months** of disciplined effort to return to fair or good credit, and longer to reach pre-incident level. Score improvement is non-linear — slow at first, then accelerating as negatives age off. Setting that expectation upfront prevents discouragement when the first three months haven't moved the score much.

## Credit-Repair Companies

A recurring client question is whether a credit-repair company can fix things faster. The honest answer is almost always no.

- **No company can legally remove accurate negative information from a credit report.** If a collection is real, a late payment actually happened, or a bankruptcy was actually filed, no service has the ability to make that information disappear. The information can only age off (7 years for most items, 10 for Chapter 7 per FCRA).
- **Everything a credit-repair company legally does, the client can do for free.** Pulling reports from the three bureaus, disputing inaccurate items, writing goodwill letters to creditors, negotiating with collectors — these are all rights the client already has.
- **Legitimate credit counseling is different from credit repair.** Nonprofit credit counseling agencies (often certified by NFCC or FCAA) provide debt management plans, budget coaching, and referrals — often for little or no cost. Substantively different from for-profit "credit repair" services.
- **Warning signs of a scam.** Demands for large upfront fees (the federal Credit Repair Organizations Act prohibits charging before services are performed). Guarantees of a specific score increase or removal of accurate information. Instructions to create a new Social Security number or Employer Identification Number to start a fresh file (this is fraud). Refusal to explain the client's rights.

The counselor's message is usually: the process takes time, there are no shortcuts, and the work isn't complicated — it's patience and consistency. A client paying $100/month for 12 months to a credit-repair company has spent $1,200 on services available for free, and has an incrementally improved score that would likely look the same with independent effort.

## Practical Application

Stage diagnosis drives the agenda:

- **Build.** First session often includes pulling all three reports (to confirm the file is thin), setting up one secured card or credit-builder loan, and explaining the 6-month timeline before the score becomes meaningful.
- **Maintain.** Usually a short conversation — set up autopay on every account, confirm old accounts are still open, check utilization, schedule a quarterly monitoring cadence. Client leaves with a maintenance calendar, not a project.
- **Rebuild.** Longest conversation. Full review of all three reports with the client. Explicit list of every negative item with a decision per item (dispute / pay / pay-for-delete request / let it age off / confirm accurate). Set up at least one new positive tradeline. Realistic 18–36 month timeline.

For an AI coach, stage diagnosis is a useful prompt structure: ask the client (or infer from data) whether there's an existing file, whether it contains negative items, and whether the client is preparing for a major application. Recommendations then branch cleanly by stage rather than delivering generic "build your credit" advice. When report data is available, the coach can run the diagnosis automatically — count tradelines, identify negative items, flag utilization — and move directly into stage-specific actions.

For users with thin files, proactive surfacing of secured-card and credit-builder-loan options from their existing bank or credit union is high-value. For users in Rebuild mode, tracking negative-item age-off dates in the background and alerting the client when a significant item is about to drop off is a concrete longitudinal move.

The coach should be explicit about what it is not — an accurate predictor of score movement. Score models are proprietary; the coach can describe directional expectations ("utilization reduction typically improves the score within 1–2 billing cycles") but should not commit to specific point changes.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Applying "Build" advice to a Maintain or Rebuild client, or vice versa.** Opening new tradelines is right in Build; usually wrong in Maintain. Diagnose first.
- **Paying a credit-repair company for work the client can do for free.** Unless the client's situation requires legal help, the DIY path is both cheaper and equally effective.
- **Expecting instant results in Rebuild.** Score improvement is non-linear and often slow in the first few months. Setting an 18–36 month realistic horizon keeps clients in the game.
- **Closing old accounts to "clean up."** Dents utilization and eventually length-of-history. Leave them open; use them lightly.
- **Running up balances "to build credit."** Utilization matters; interest accrual does not build anything. Pay in full every month.
- **Reaffirming old debt without checking the SOL.** A payment on a stale debt can reset the clock in some states and reopen legal exposure that had expired. See debt-default-and-collections before paying on old collector contacts.
- **Missing the autopay setup during Maintain.** One missed due date on a long-clean account can cost more score than 12 months of careful behavior added.
