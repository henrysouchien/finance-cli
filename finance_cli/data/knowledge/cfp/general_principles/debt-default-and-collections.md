# Debt Default and Collections

Default is not a single event; it is a staged process. The client who stops making payments this month is not in the same legal position as the client whose debt was charged off twelve months ago and sold to a collections agency, who is not in the same position as the client with a court judgment and wage garnishment pending. Each stage carries different risks, different options, and different legal protections.

A client arriving at coaching while in active collections often does not know where they are in the process. The first diagnostic is to figure out which stage is active — and where the client has tools the collector or creditor may not have volunteered.

This topic is **`scope: framing_only`**. The coach educates on the staged process, the federal protections, and the rights the client can exercise on their own. Specific legal decisions — whether to respond to a particular lawsuit, which exemptions to claim on a garnishment, whether bankruptcy is appropriate — route to a consumer-rights attorney or bankruptcy attorney. See `general_principles.consumer-credit-types` for the broader credit taxonomy this topic builds on.

## The Escalation Stages

Default progresses through these stages:

1. **Late payment (day 1).** Payment missed; late fee applies; creditor sends reminder.
2. **30 days late.** Reported to credit bureaus. First significant score drop.
3. **60 days late.** More aggressive collection activity from the original creditor. Second score hit.
4. **90 days late.** Further score damage; creditor may start sending pre-charge-off notices.
5. **120–180 days late (varies by product).** The creditor "**charges off**" the debt — a write-down on their books. The debt is still owed; the charge-off is the creditor's accounting recognition that they don't expect to collect. Most unsecured debts (credit cards) charge off around day 180.
6. **Post-charge-off.** Original creditor either continues collecting internally, hires a third-party collection agency, or sells the debt to a debt buyer. The "collections" phase begins.
7. **Collection activity.** Letters, phone calls, possible lawsuit threats. FDCPA governs what the collector can do.
8. **Lawsuit.** If unresolved, the creditor or debt buyer may sue. If the debtor doesn't respond, a default judgment is typically entered.
9. **Judgment and post-judgment remedies.** Wage garnishment, bank levy, judgment lien, depending on state law.

The client's options change at each stage. Working with the original creditor is usually easier than working with a collection agency; working with a collection agency is easier than working with a debt buyer; once a judgment is entered, options narrow substantially.

## The Two Federal Statutes

Federal law provides two separate frameworks:

### Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)

Governs how consumer credit information is collected, maintained, accessed, and corrected. Relevant to default and collections through:

- **7-year reporting window.** Most negative information stays on the credit report for **7 years from the date of the original delinquency** — not 7 years from the most recent activity. A debt that went delinquent in March 2020 ages off in March 2027 regardless of how many collection agencies have handled it.
- **10-year window for Chapter 7 bankruptcy.** Longer than other negatives.
- **Dispute rights.** The consumer can dispute inaccurate items; the bureau must investigate within 30–45 days.
- **Re-aging prohibition.** Collectors cannot reset the original delinquency date by transferring the debt. A client who sees a collection listing a more recent "date of delinquency" than the actual original delinquency has grounds for a dispute.

### Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA)

Regulates **third-party debt collectors** — agencies that collect debts on behalf of others, and debt buyers who purchased debts they didn't originate. Original creditors collecting their own debts are not covered by FDCPA (state equivalents may apply).

Key rules:

- **No contact at unusual hours.** Generally before 8am or after 9pm in the debtor's local time.
- **No contact at work** if the debtor has informed the collector that the employer prohibits such contact.
- **No harassment, threats, or deception.** Specific prohibitions on threatening legal action the collector isn't actually prepared to take, falsely implying the debtor will go to jail, misrepresenting the amount owed, or using obscene or threatening language.
- **No third-party disclosure.** The collector generally cannot discuss the debt with anyone other than the debtor (narrow exception for locating the debtor).
- **Cease-contact rights.** The debtor can send a written demand that the collector stop contacting them. The collector must then limit further contact to a single notice stating the specific action they will take.
- **Debt validation.** First collector contact must include a validation letter (see below).

Both statutes are federal rights, not favors. Violations can be reported to the CFPB, the FTC, or the state attorney general, and create private rights of action.

## The Debt Validation Letter

When a collector first contacts the debtor, they must (within 5 days of initial communication) send a written notice stating:

- Amount of the debt.
- Name of the creditor to whom the debt is owed.
- That the debtor has 30 days to dispute the debt's validity.
- That if disputed in writing within 30 days, the collector will obtain verification and mail it.
- That if requested within 30 days, the collector will provide the original creditor's name and address if different.

What a debtor does with this:

- **If the debt is unfamiliar, disputed, or stale:** send a written dispute within 30 days demanding validation. The collector must cease collection activity until they provide verification — which, for old debts that have been transferred through multiple buyers, often means providing records they don't actually have.
- **If the debt is recognized and real:** the validation letter still establishes a paper trail of who owns the debt, how much they claim, and from when.

Some older debts cannot be validated by the current collector because the records were lost or not transferred in the sale. A debtor who disputes and demands validation sometimes causes the collector to withdraw — not because the debt wasn't real, but because the collector cannot prove it.

## Statute of Limitations

Each state has a **statute of limitations (SOL)** on debt collection lawsuits. After the SOL expires (typically 3–6 years for most consumer debts, varying by state and debt type), the creditor can no longer sue to collect. The debt still exists; the legal tool of the lawsuit is off the table.

Critical subtleties:

- **SOL is an affirmative defense.** A debtor sued on a time-barred debt still has to show up in court and raise SOL as a defense. If they do not appear, a default judgment can be entered even on a time-barred debt.
- **The clock typically runs from the date of last activity** — usually the date of last payment. In some states, acknowledgment of the debt in writing or a new payment can **restart the clock** — making a time-barred debt enforceable again.
- **Reaffirming old debt is dangerous.** A collector calling about a debt 5 years old is often probing to see if the debtor will make a payment or acknowledge the debt in writing. The payment or acknowledgment resets the SOL.
- **Credit-report duration is separate from SOL.** A debt may be past its SOL (cannot be sued on) but still within its 7-year credit-report window. The two clocks are independent.

A client contacted about an old debt should look up their state's SOL before doing anything else. If the debt is time-barred, the legal risk of not paying is limited to credit-report damage (which is already happening). The risk of paying or acknowledging is restarting legal exposure.

## Judgments and Post-Judgment Remedies

When a creditor wins a collection lawsuit, the court enters a **judgment** — a legal ruling that the debtor owes the specified amount. A judgment unlocks collection tools that were not available before:

- **Wage garnishment.** Court order directing the debtor's employer to withhold a portion of each paycheck. **Up to 25%** of disposable income under the federal Consumer Credit Protection Act (CCPA) cap; states may set lower caps. Certain income is **exempt**: Social Security benefits, unemployment insurance, workers' compensation, public assistance, student financial aid, VA benefits, and in some states other categories.
- **Bank levy.** Court order directing the debtor's bank to freeze and transfer funds. Federal benefits electronically deposited are protected by specific rules.
- **Property lien (judgment lien).** Court-approved claim on real estate. If the property is sold, the lien must be satisfied from the proceeds before the debtor receives anything. In some states, a judgment lien can eventually force a sale.

A judgment stays on the credit report for **7 years from the judgment date**, and the underlying lien can be renewed by the creditor periodically.

Post-judgment work — claiming exemptions, negotiating payment plans, evaluating bankruptcy — is firmly attorney territory. The coach's role is to flag the urgency and route.

## Secured Debt Default: Repossession and Foreclosure

Default on secured debt has a different dynamic than unsecured default — the lender has a specific asset to go after without first obtaining a judgment.

- **Auto loan default → repossession.** Most state laws allow the lender to repossess the vehicle without a court order, often without warning, once the borrower is in default. The vehicle is typically sold at auction; sale proceeds are applied to the loan balance; the borrower remains liable for any **deficiency** (the gap between sale price and balance plus repossession and auction costs). The deficiency becomes unsecured debt subject to the same collection process as any other unsecured debt.
- **Mortgage default → foreclosure.** A more regulated process with required notices, waiting periods, and (in most states) court involvement. Typical timeline from first missed mortgage payment to foreclosure sale is 6 months to 2 years, varying widely by state. Many mortgages include loss-mitigation options (modification, short sale, deed-in-lieu) that a struggling borrower should pursue early.

For both, the imperative is **early intervention**. Lenders are usually more flexible when the borrower is early in delinquency and proactive about communication. Once repossession or foreclosure is underway, options narrow.

## Debt Collection Scams

A recurring fraud pattern targets people who are or might be in debt:

- **Calls claiming to collect a debt the target doesn't actually owe.** Often using publicly available information to sound legitimate.
- **Threats of arrest or immediate legal action.** No debt collector can have someone arrested for non-payment of civil debt in the United States. Threatening arrest is itself an FDCPA violation.
- **Demanding payment via gift cards, wire transfer, or cryptocurrency.** Legitimate collectors accept normal payment methods. Unusual payment demands are almost always scams.
- **Refusing to provide validation.** A legitimate collector must provide the validation letter described above. A caller who refuses to put anything in writing is almost certainly running a scam.
- **Calling with incomplete or inconsistent information.** Unknown original creditor, vague account numbers, inconsistent amounts owed across calls.

Defenses:

- **Never provide personal information to an unsolicited collection call.** Ask for the debt to be validated in writing.
- **Request the collector's identity** — full business name, mailing address, license to collect in the target's state.
- **Check the debt independently** through a credit-report pull.
- **Report scams** to the FTC, CFPB, or state attorney general.
- Real collectors must follow FDCPA. A caller violating FDCPA is either a scammer or a legitimate collector who can be reported.

## Hand-Off Discipline

This topic is in coaching scope but specific decisions route out:

- **A lawsuit has been filed against the client.** Response window is typically 20–30 days (state-specific). Missing the deadline produces a default judgment. Coach flags the urgency loudly and routes to legal aid, a consumer-rights attorney, or court-based self-help. Coach does not draft the response.
- **A judgment has been entered with garnishment or levy pending.** Exemption claims (Social Security, UI, WC, VA benefits, state-specific protections) require legal-aid or attorney involvement. Coach explains the exemption framework; specific filings route to counsel.
- **Bankruptcy is being considered** as a response to active collections. The bankruptcy decision is bankruptcy-attorney territory. Coach explains the framework and indicates the conditions where bankruptcy becomes seriously considered (multiple judgments, garnishment, large medical debt, certain post-disaster scenarios). Specific Chapter selection and filing decisions go to counsel.
- **Stale-debt SOL questions.** Coach can explain the SOL framework and the reaffirmation risk. Specific state-SOL determination (which clock applies, whether the clock has run) for an enforcement decision routes to legal aid or attorney.
- **FDCPA private-action decisions.** A client whose collector has violated FDCPA may have a private right of action. Coach helps document violations; pursuit of a private claim routes to a consumer-rights attorney.

The coach's posture across all of these: **educate on rights and process, flag stage-specific urgency, refer to appropriate resources.** Don't try to be the legal help; be the resource router.

## Practical Application

Counseling moments:

- **Early delinquency.** Highest-leverage moment. Restructuring, hardship programs, and direct negotiation with the original creditor are cheaper and easier now than after charge-off. Encourage the client to call the creditor before the next missed payment.
- **Post-charge-off contact.** Map where the debt went — original creditor still trying to collect, assigned to a collection agency, or sold to a debt buyer. Settlement pricing varies by where the debt is in this chain; debt buyers often accept pennies on the dollar because they bought it for pennies.
- **First contact from a collector.** Request validation in writing. Don't acknowledge or pay anything until validation is received and verified.
- **Stale-debt contact.** Check state SOL before any acknowledgment. If the debt is time-barred, the risk calculus changes completely.
- **Lawsuit filed.** Client must respond — default judgments are the single most common way unrepresented debtors lose cases they could have defended. Even an imperfect response is dramatically better than no response. Route to legal aid or attorney.
- **Judgment entered.** Garnishment, levy, lien possible. Review exemptions. Bankruptcy becomes seriously considered. Route to counsel.
- **Harassment by collectors.** Document every contact (date, time, caller identity, what was said). Violations of FDCPA provide the basis for complaints and sometimes private lawsuits.

For an AI coach, default-and-collections content needs careful handling. The coach can explain structure, walk through client rights, and flag when legal help is needed, but specific legal decisions are firmly in attorney territory. The coach can help **document collector contacts** — a log of calls (time, stated identity, content) is useful evidence if an FDCPA complaint is filed. The coach can also **track SOL clocks and credit-report age-off dates** for the client across active accounts.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Ignoring contact from collectors.** Avoidance does not make the debt go away; it often turns a negotiable situation into a judgment situation.
- **Paying a scam collector.** Once money is sent via gift card or wire, it's gone. Verify any debt independently.
- **Acknowledging a time-barred debt without checking state SOL.** Can restart the clock and restore legal exposure that had expired.
- **Not responding to a lawsuit.** Default judgments are the single most common way unrepresented debtors lose cases. Even an imperfect response is dramatically better than no response.
- **Missing exemptions on garnishment.** Federal benefits (Social Security, SSI, VA benefits) have specific protections that a client may have to affirmatively claim. Legal help is valuable at this stage.
- **Negotiating settlements without understanding tax implications.** Forgiven debt above $600 typically produces a 1099-C and taxable income.
- **Making a partial payment thinking it will satisfy the debt.** Partial payments usually don't satisfy the debt in full unless an explicit settlement agreement is in place in writing.
- **Not saving the FDCPA paper trail.** Collector violations create rights only if documented. A client experiencing harassment should log every contact.
- **Letting a judgment sit unaddressed.** Judgments have their own 7-year credit-report horizon that runs separately from the original debt's horizon, and they renew. Proactive resolution is usually better than passive exposure.
