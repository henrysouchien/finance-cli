# Home Affordability

Home affordability is the pre-purchase math that answers a narrower question than "will a lender approve me?" The lender's maximum is a credit decision; the household's healthy number is a stability decision. The coach's role is to frame the full cost, expose the tradeoffs, and help the user decide what to fix first before shopping.

This topic supports readiness and education. It does not approve a mortgage, quote live rates from memory, recommend a specific loan product, or replace lender underwriting.

## Key Concepts

**PITI is the starting number, not the whole number.** A mortgage payment includes principal, interest, property taxes, and homeowners insurance. HOA fees, mortgage insurance, and other mandatory housing costs sit alongside PITI. A readiness conversation should not compare income to principal-and-interest alone.

**Maintenance reserve is part of ownership cost.** AFCPE frames maintenance and repairs as a recurring ownership obligation. A practical planning range is 1-2% of home value per year, accumulated monthly even if the spending is irregular.

**Front-end ratio.**

```text
front_end_ratio = (PITI + HOA) / gross_monthly_income
```

The traditional conventional anchor is about 28%. A user may choose a lower comfort target, such as 25%, if they value more monthly margin.

**Back-end ratio.**

```text
back_end_ratio = (PITI + HOA + other_monthly_debt_payments) / gross_monthly_income
```

The traditional conventional anchor is about 36%. Higher approvals can happen, especially with compensating factors or government-backed programs, but "approvable" and "wise for this household" are different.

**Cash to close is not just the down payment.** The buyer needs down payment, closing costs, moving costs, immediate-post-close costs, and a protected reserve after closing. Closing costs commonly run 2-5% of purchase price, but the actual number comes from the lender and settlement process.

**Post-close reserves are a hard readiness check.** A user who can reach the down payment only by draining emergency savings is not in the same position as a user with separate down payment and reserve buckets.

**PMI changes the monthly cost.** Conventional borrowers below 20% equity usually pay private mortgage insurance. PMI protects the lender, not the borrower. Under federal PMI-cancellation rules for covered loans, borrower-requested cancellation is tied to the 80% scheduled principal-balance point if other requirements are met; automatic termination generally comes later. The user should verify the exact rules with the servicer and disclosures.

**Credit readiness affects the rate.** Credit score bands, utilization, hard inquiries, and report errors can change the rate and monthly payment. Before a major application, the user should review all three credit reports through `referrals.annualcreditreport` and apply the Build/Maintain/Rebuild framework from `general_principles.building-credit`.

## Practical Application

The skill should build scenarios from explicit assumptions:

- purchase price;
- down payment;
- loan amount;
- rate and source of rate assumption;
- term;
- property tax estimate;
- homeowners insurance estimate;
- HOA;
- PMI or mortgage-insurance estimate;
- maintenance reserve;
- other monthly debt payments;
- liquid cash and reserve target.

Show at least three numbers separately:

- **monthly housing payment:** PITI + HOA + PMI or required mortgage insurance;
- **full monthly homeownership cost:** monthly housing payment + maintenance reserve;
- **cash-to-close gap:** down payment + closing + moving/immediate costs minus available cash after preserving reserves.

Then classify the readiness result:

- **education_only:** the user is exploring and not ready for a decision;
- **not_ready:** one or more hard constraints are missing;
- **fix_first:** homebuying is plausible after specific debt, cash, credit, or reserve work;
- **preapproval_ready:** the user has enough evidence to speak with a lender or HUD-approved counselor;
- **refer:** the situation needs a housing counselor, lender, attorney, or other specialist before the coach continues.

## Hand-Off Discipline

The coach may educate, calculate scenarios, and help the user identify readiness gaps. The coach must hand off when the task crosses into approval, product selection, legal interpretation, or insurance placement.

- Do not guarantee approval or state that a user qualifies for a particular mortgage.
- Do not quote current mortgage rates from memory; ask for a user-provided rate, lender quote, or current-source lookup.
- Do not recommend a specific lender, real estate agent, loan product, or insurer.
- Do not decide down-payment-assistance eligibility; route to `referrals.hud-approved-housing-counselor` or the program administrator.
- Do not sell or place homeowners insurance; route policy selection to an insurance professional.
- Do not interpret contract, title, inspection, or closing legal issues; route to the appropriate professional.

## Common Pitfalls

- Comparing rent to principal-and-interest only.
- Treating the preapproval maximum as the target purchase price.
- Ignoring tax, insurance, HOA, PMI, or maintenance reserve.
- Counting the emergency fund as down-payment money.
- Saving for down payment but not closing costs.
- Assuming PMI protects the borrower.
- Assuming PMI disappears automatically at the first 20% equity estimate.
- Applying for new credit shortly before a mortgage application.
- Using a stale credit report or a score estimate without checking report contents.
