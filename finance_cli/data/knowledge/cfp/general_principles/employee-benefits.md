# Employee Benefits

Employee benefits are non-wage compensation and protections that a worker receives or elects through an employer. They turn abstract planning into payroll deductions, coverage elections, match formulas, vesting schedules, and enrollment deadlines.

This topic supports contribution-readiness coaching by framing employer match, payroll contribution mechanics, open enrollment, HSA/FSA choices, and plan-provider routing. It does not recommend a specific insurance policy, investment fund, retirement-plan election, or legal/tax position.

## Key Concepts

**Total compensation is broader than salary.** A job offer or benefits package can include health coverage, retirement match, disability coverage, life insurance, paid time off, EAP services, commuter benefits, stock compensation, and other programs. A lower salary with stronger benefits can be economically stronger than a higher salary with weak benefits.

**Payroll makes benefits concrete.** Retirement contributions, health premiums, HSA/FSA elections, and some insurance premiums appear as paycheck deductions. The contribution-readiness skill should connect any proposed increase to net-pay and spending-plan impact rather than only to gross salary.

**Employer match is compensation.** A match formula such as "100% up to 3%" or "50% up to 6%" means the employer contributes when the employee contributes. Missing the full match is usually leaving compensation unclaimed. The exact formula, per-pay-period behavior, true-up policy, and vesting schedule come from HR or the plan provider.

**Vesting affects job-change timing.** Employee contributions are owned by the employee. Employer contributions may vest over time. A user near a vesting cliff should verify the schedule before making job-change decisions that forfeit employer money.

**Open enrollment is a decision window.** Health plans, HSA/FSA elections, disability coverage, life insurance, and other benefits often lock for the plan year unless a qualifying life event occurs. The coach can help the user gather documents and compare tradeoffs before the deadline.

**HSA requires eligibility facts.** A high-deductible health plan can unlock HSA contributions, but the user must confirm HSA-eligible HDHP coverage and no disqualifying coverage. HSA contribution readiness belongs in this benefits frame before it belongs in a retirement contribution sequence.

**FSA is not HSA.** Flexible Spending Accounts can be valuable pre-tax tools but are usually use-it-or-lose-it and employer-controlled. They should not be treated as long-horizon retirement savings.

## Contribution Readiness Use

When `coach_retirement_contribution_readiness` reads or asks for benefits facts, it should gather:

- employer plan type and whether Roth and traditional contributions are available;
- employee contribution rate or amount;
- employer match formula, true-up behavior, and vesting schedule;
- salary or eligible compensation used by the plan;
- current-year employee contributions to date;
- HDHP/HSA eligibility, coverage type, and year-to-date HSA contributions;
- enrollment or payroll-change deadline.

If any of these are unknown, classify the missing input as a data gap instead of estimating. A reasonable next action may be "open the benefits summary" or "ask HR/plan provider for the match formula" rather than "increase contributions now."

## Hand-Off Discipline

The coach may explain benefits vocabulary, compute draft paycheck impact, and prepare questions for HR or a provider. Route out for:

- insurance product selection or rider comparison;
- stock-option, ESPP, RSU, or deferred-compensation tax strategy;
- plan-document disputes, denied claims, vesting disputes, QDROs, or legal interpretations;
- complex self-employed plan selection;
- tax filing treatment or correction of excess contributions.

## Common Pitfalls

- Comparing job offers on salary alone.
- Ignoring the match formula or assuming the employer contributes automatically.
- Increasing payroll contributions without checking monthly cash-flow impact.
- Missing open enrollment and keeping stale defaults.
- Treating an FSA as portable or as a long-term savings account.
- Choosing an HDHP for lower premium but never opening or funding the HSA.
- Leaving a job shortly before material employer contributions vest.
