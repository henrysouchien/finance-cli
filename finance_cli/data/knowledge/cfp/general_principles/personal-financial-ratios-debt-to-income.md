# Debt-to-Income (DTI) Ratio

The debt-to-income ratio (DTI) compares a household's monthly debt-payment obligations to its income. It is one of two foundational personal financial ratios (the sibling is liquidity — see `general_principles.personal-financial-ratios.liquidity`). Lenders use variations of DTI when approving or denying mortgage applications, which means DTI is a number most clients will encounter eventually whether they track it personally or not.

This topic is the DTI half of the personal-financial-ratios wiki article; liquidity is distilled separately because the two ratios trigger different intervention patterns (DTI → debt-planning interventions; liquidity → emergency-fund interventions) per the wiki-to-topic 1:N cardinality rule.

## Key Concepts

**Formula.**

```
DTI = Total Debt Payments / Gross Income
```

Both sides use the same time basis — typically monthly for personal calculation, annual for lender quotes. The denominator uses **gross income** (pre-tax), not the net figure that sits on the cash flow statement. This is the one place in personal-finance practice where the convention explicitly wants gross numbers; the choice matches lender calculations.

**What counts in the numerator.** Minimum required payments on all debts:

- Mortgage or rent (lenders include mortgage; many personal calculations include rent too)
- Auto loans
- Student loans
- Credit-card minimum payments
- Any other scheduled debt service

What does NOT count: utilities, subscriptions, groceries, or other non-debt expenses. Those are budget line items, not debt obligations.

**Front-end vs. back-end DTI.** Mortgage applications produce two figures:

- **Front-end DTI** — housing costs only (PITI: principal, interest, taxes, insurance) divided by gross income. Often targeted at 28% or below.
- **Back-end DTI** — all debt service (housing plus other monthly debt payments) divided by gross income. The benchmarks below apply to back-end DTI.

The personal-planning version of DTI is back-end. Track both when the client is shopping for a mortgage.

## Benchmarks

The following are widely-cited lender conventions, not AFCPE-specific guidance:

- **~36% or below** — healthy personal DTI; comfortable for most lenders.
- **36–43%** — typical conforming-mortgage ceiling; many lenders use this band as their approval cutoff.
- **Over 43%** — elevated; mortgage approval becomes difficult.

Treat these as directional ranges. Individual lender underwriting and program-specific (FHA, VA, USDA) thresholds vary.

## Worked Example

A two-earner family with: full-time worker $5,555/month gross, part-time worker $2,085/month gross, plus $800/month pre-tax investment income. Monthly gross total: $8,440. Annualized: $101,280.

Monthly debt payments: $485 student loan + $150 credit-card minimum + $165 auto loan = $800. Annualized: $9,600.

```
DTI = $9,600 / $101,280 ≈ 0.095 or 9.5%
```

A DTI under 10% is well within the comfortable range. The household has substantial headroom for additional debt service if needed (though additional debt is not a goal in itself).

## Interpretation

- **DTI is a backward-looking input constraint.** It describes the current debt load relative to current earnings; it does not say whether the debt is strategic or wasteful.
- **Two levers for lowering DTI.** Reduce debt payments (pay off balances, refinance to lower rates) or increase gross income. Both work; the cash flow statement reveals which lever is actually available.
- **Lenders compute it differently.** Some lenders include rent; some weight different debt types differently; program-specific (FHA, VA) thresholds vary. The personal-finance version above is the orienting calculation, not a lender-specific number.

## Practical Application

For an AI coach, DTI is a diagnostic to surface alongside the cash flow statement. Once gross-income and debt-payment data are available from transaction records and account balances, DTI computes automatically. Display the ratio with context — not "DTI: 18%" in isolation, but "DTI: 18% — well within the range most lenders consider healthy; a mortgage shouldn't be a DTI problem for you." Show the reading, show the benchmark, explain what it means. Avoid false precision — DTI is an orienting number, not tax-form exactitude.

DTI is the first ratio to compute when the client is preparing for a mortgage application, considering taking on additional debt, or trying to assess whether their current debt load is sustainable. The benchmark anchors the conversation; the actual coaching work is helping the client decide which lever to pull if DTI is elevated.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Using net income.** DTI convention uses gross. Mixing in net produces a misleading number that won't match what a lender sees.
- **Including non-debt expenses.** Groceries, utilities, and subscriptions are budget items, not debt obligations. They don't belong in the DTI numerator.
- **Treating the benchmark as a goal.** "Keep DTI below 36%" is an orienting range, not an exact target. Individual circumstances matter more than hitting a round number.
- **Computing once.** DTI is a snapshot. Recompute when gross income or debt service changes — monthly for working households, quarterly at minimum.
- **Confusing front-end with back-end.** They are different numbers with different benchmarks. Always know which one is being quoted.
