# Bankruptcy

Bankruptcy is the most consequential legal option in consumer debt management, and the one most surrounded by stigma. A client considering it often arrives having already tried to solve their debt without it, exhausted informal options, and accumulated shame alongside the debt. The role of coaching is both practical and rhetorical: practical in walking through the mechanics, rhetorical in reframing bankruptcy as a legal instrument designed for specific situations — not a moral failing, not a last-resort admission of defeat, but a tool with clear use cases and clear tradeoffs.

This topic is **`scope: framing_only`**. The coach explains structure, surfaces diagnostic signals, supports post-discharge rebuilding, and routes to a bankruptcy attorney. Filing decisions and chapter selection are firmly attorney territory. See `general_principles.debt-default-and-collections` for the path that typically leads to the bankruptcy decision.

The topic carries a secondary `cfp_domain: tax` because the tax-treatment of discharged debt — bankruptcy-discharged debt is excluded from gross income under IRC §108, an important exception to the general 1099-C taxable-forgiven-debt rule — is consequential enough to surface here. Primary domain remains `general_principles` per the A2 fundamental-category rule (this is consumer-rights/debt content; tax treatment is a downstream consequence).

## The Two Consumer Chapters

Federal bankruptcy law defines several chapters; for individual consumers, the two most common are Chapter 7 and Chapter 13.

### Chapter 7 — Liquidation Bankruptcy

The faster, simpler path. A Chapter 7 filing:

- **Liquidates non-exempt assets.** A court-appointed trustee evaluates the debtor's property, identifies exempt assets (protected under federal or state exemption statutes — typically a primary residence up to a value cap, vehicle up to a cap, tools of the trade, household goods, retirement accounts, and certain personal property), and sells non-exempt assets to repay creditors. In practice, most Chapter 7 cases are **"no-asset" cases** — the debtor's property falls entirely within exemptions, and nothing is liquidated.
- **Discharges most unsecured debt.** After trustee administration completes (typically 3–6 months from filing), dischargeable debts are eliminated.
- **Stays on the credit report for 10 years** from the filing date per FCRA.

Eligibility is subject to a **means test** under BAPCPA — the debtor's income compared to the state median, and (if above median) an analysis of disposable income available for repayment. A debtor with enough income to repay a meaningful portion of debt may be directed to Chapter 13 instead.

Right fit when:

- Unsecured debt (credit cards, medical bills, personal loans, deficiency balances) is the primary problem.
- Current income cannot realistically service the debt through a repayment plan.
- The debtor has few non-exempt assets that would be lost in liquidation.

### Chapter 13 — Restructuring Bankruptcy

The slower, structured path. A Chapter 13 filing:

- **Creates a 3–5 year repayment plan.** The debtor commits to paying a portion of their income to creditors through a trustee, according to a court-approved plan.
- **Allows the debtor to keep their property** — including non-exempt assets — by paying the value of non-exempt assets to creditors through the plan rather than surrendering them.
- **Can "cram down" certain secured debts.** For some assets (not the primary residence), the loan balance can be reduced to current asset value, with the remainder treated as unsecured. Useful when the debtor owes more than an asset is worth.
- **Can cure mortgage arrears.** A debtor behind on mortgage payments can catch up through the plan, stopping foreclosure and preserving the home.
- **Discharges remaining unsecured balances at plan completion.** After 3–5 years of plan payments, unsecured debts beyond what was paid are discharged.
- **Stays on the credit report for 7 years** from the filing date — shorter than Chapter 7.

Right fit when:

- The debtor has steady income sufficient to support a repayment plan.
- There are secured assets (primarily a home) the debtor wants to keep despite arrears.
- Non-exempt assets exist that would be lost under Chapter 7.
- The debtor was disqualified from Chapter 7 by the means test.

### Side-by-Side

| Feature | Chapter 7 | Chapter 13 |
|---|---|---|
| Nickname | Liquidation | Wage Earner's / Restructuring |
| Timeline | 3–6 months | 3–5 year repayment plan |
| Property | Non-exempt assets may be sold by trustee | Keep property; pay unsecured creditors at least the value of non-exempt assets |
| Repayment | Minimal to none | Structured plan; some partial repayment |
| Credit report duration | 10 years | 7 years |
| Typical fit | Low income, mostly unsecured debt, few non-exempt assets | Steady income, want to keep property, have arrears to cure |

## Mechanisms Applying to Both Chapters

### Automatic Stay

The moment a bankruptcy case is filed, an **automatic stay** goes into effect — a federal legal protection that halts most collection activity by creditors. Lawsuits pause. Wage garnishments stop. Foreclosure and repossession actions are suspended. Collection calls and letters must cease.

The stay is not absolute (criminal prosecutions, domestic support proceedings, some regulatory actions are exempt), but for the typical consumer debt landscape it is broad and immediate. The stay is often the most urgent reason a client files — a debtor facing garnishment next week, foreclosure sale next month, or imminent bank levy can get breathing room by filing today.

### The Trustee

Each case has a **bankruptcy trustee** appointed by the court to administer the case. In Chapter 7, the trustee evaluates assets, handles liquidation of non-exempt property, and distributes proceeds to creditors. In Chapter 13, the trustee receives the debtor's plan payments and distributes them.

The debtor interacts with the trustee at a **341 meeting of creditors** — a required procedural meeting where the trustee asks standard questions about assets, income, and financial situation. Creditors can attend but rarely do; for most consumer cases, the 341 meeting is routine.

### Discharge

The **discharge** is the court order legally releasing the debtor from obligations on dischargeable debts. In Chapter 7, discharge typically issues at the end of the 3-to-6-month administration. In Chapter 13, discharge issues at successful completion of the repayment plan. Post-discharge, creditors holding discharged debts cannot collect on them.

### Dismissal

A **dismissal** is termination of the case without a discharge — debts remain, automatic stay ends. Can happen for procedural failures (missing filings, failure to attend 341 meeting, failure to make Chapter 13 plan payments), abusive filing, or at the debtor's request.

## Discharge Mechanics

Not all debts are dischargeable. The non-dischargeable categories have grown through legislative changes; the list includes:

- **Most tax debts.** Exception for certain older income tax liabilities meeting specific age and filing criteria.
- **Child support and alimony** (domestic support obligations).
- **Court restitution orders and criminal fines.**
- **Student loans** — both federal and private. Technically dischargeable with a hardship finding, but the standard is difficult and most student-loan debt survives bankruptcy.
- **Debts incurred by fraud**, if challenged by the creditor.
- **Debts from willful and malicious injury**, if challenged by the creditor.
- **Debts for personal injury or death caused by driving under the influence.**
- **Debts incurred after filing** (post-petition debts).
- **Certain government-imposed debts** (some fines and penalties).

Secured debts have nuanced treatment. The personal obligation to repay can be discharged, but **the lender's lien on the collateral survives**. A debtor with a mortgage discharged in Chapter 7 who stops paying loses the home to foreclosure — discharge extinguished the personal obligation to pay the mortgage, not the lender's right to take the house.

### Reaffirmation

**Reaffirming** a debt is a formal agreement to remain liable on a specific debt that would otherwise be discharged. Common for a car loan the debtor wants to keep paying, or sometimes for specific credit-card relationships. Once reaffirmed, the debt is no longer part of the discharge.

Reaffirmation should not be entered lightly. The court-required analysis considers whether the debtor can afford the payments going forward — reaffirming a car loan the debtor cannot actually afford reproduces the original problem with less legal cushion.

### Exempt Property

Assets protected from creditors during bankruptcy. Exemptions are established by statute — federal exemptions exist, and each state has its own; debtors in most states can choose which system to use, though some states require state exemptions.

Common categories:

- **Homestead exemption** on the primary residence, up to a value cap.
- **Vehicle exemption** up to a value cap.
- **Tools of the trade** (work equipment).
- **Household goods and furnishings.**
- **Retirement accounts** — broadly protected under federal law. ERISA-qualified employer plans (401(k), pension) are protected under ERISA; IRA bankruptcy protection is a separate mechanism under the Bankruptcy Code §522(b)(3)(C) and §522(n), with a BAPCPA-set inflation-adjusted exemption cap.
- **Social Security benefits, disability, and public assistance.**
- **Wildcard exemption** (an amount of any property the debtor chooses, up to a cap) in some systems.

Exemption amounts change on regulatory schedules; specific dollar figures belong in current-year reference material (see Effective-Date Notice below).

## Tax Treatment of Discharged Debt

A useful exception to a general rule. Forgiven debt is normally taxable income — a creditor that cancels $5,000 of a credit card balance typically issues a 1099-C, and the debtor reports the $5,000 as income on their return.

**Bankruptcy-discharged debt is excluded from gross income under IRC §108.** A debtor who discharges $50,000 of unsecured debt in Chapter 7 does not owe income tax on the $50,000. This is a substantial advantage over informal debt forgiveness or settlement, where the tax bill on cancelled debt can produce a second financial crisis on top of the original debt problem.

The §108 exclusion also covers some non-bankruptcy scenarios (insolvency exclusion, certain qualified-principal-residence indebtedness exclusions on a separate timeline) but bankruptcy is the cleanest case. A client weighing settlement versus bankruptcy should factor the tax treatment into the math.

## The Credit Impact

Filing for bankruptcy is a significant credit event but not the end-of-credit event clients often expect:

- **Chapter 7** stays on the credit report for **10 years** from the filing date per FCRA.
- **Chapter 13** stays for **7 years** from the filing date per FCRA.
- **Score impact** is steep at the filing moment — scores can drop 130–200 points for previously-good credit, less for already-low scores (where the score was already capturing the underlying delinquencies).
- **Rebuilding can begin immediately.** Secured cards and credit-builder loans are accessible post-filing; some lenders specifically market to recently-discharged debtors. The Rebuild playbook applies; new on-time positive data outweighs the bankruptcy mark over time, and the score often recovers meaningfully within 2–3 years.
- **Mortgage eligibility** typically requires 2–4 years post-discharge, depending on the loan program. FHA and VA have shorter waiting periods than conventional; extenuating circumstances (medical events, job loss beyond the debtor's control) can shorten the wait.

The honest framing: a client considering bankruptcy is often already living with severely damaged credit from the pre-filing delinquencies. The marginal credit damage from the filing itself may be less than clients fear, and the post-filing trajectory (especially with disciplined Rebuild) is usually faster than continuing to struggle with unmanageable debt.

## Diagnostic Signals — When the Math Has Run Out

Signs that reduction strategies cannot close the math:

- **Total unsecured debt exceeds 50% of annual gross income**, with minimum payments consuming a large share of monthly income.
- **Interest charges alone exceed the amount the client can commit to debt repayment.** Payoff under any reduction strategy is essentially impossible — the balance grows faster than payments reduce it.
- **Projected payoff timeline exceeds the client's working years.** A 50-year-old facing a 35-year debt-free projection is on a path that doesn't make financial sense.
- **The client is being sued, facing garnishment or levy**, and has no realistic path to settle or pay the judgments.
- **The client is facing foreclosure** and cannot negotiate a workable modification.
- **Continued struggle is producing ongoing harm that compounds** — new delinquencies, new collections, new fees — and the client has no realistic path to stabilize without legal intervention.

None of these individually means bankruptcy is the answer. Taken together, or individually in severe forms, they signal that reduction strategies cannot close the math.

Reverse signals — reasons bankruptcy is NOT the right tool:

- **Strong income stability with high disposable income** that could support a debt management plan or consolidation.
- **Mostly secured debt the client wants to keep.** Direct negotiation with lenders (modification, refinance) is often a better first move.
- **Debts that are mostly non-dischargeable** (recent taxes, student loans, support obligations). Bankruptcy doesn't help with the debts that are the actual problem.
- **The underlying spending pattern is not stabilized.** A client with a spending-discipline issue who discharges debt without addressing the underlying behavior typically rebuilds the problem — and a second bankruptcy has longer waiting periods and more scrutiny.

## Hand-Off Discipline

This topic is in coaching scope but specific decisions route out:

- **The filing decision itself.** Whether to file, which Chapter, when. Bankruptcy attorneys consult on this; the coach surfaces the option and the diagnostic signals. Initial consultations are often free or low-cost.
- **Chapter selection.** Coach explains the structural difference between Chapter 7 and Chapter 13. Specific Chapter selection — including means-test analysis — is attorney work.
- **Petition preparation.** The bankruptcy petition requires complete disclosure of income, assets, debts, and recent financial activity. This is specialist work; errors can cause denial of discharge.
- **Pre-filing conduct.** Some actions in the months before filing (preferential payments to insiders, luxury purchases on credit, asset transfers) can cause problems. A client who has decided to file should work with their attorney before taking any unusual financial actions.
- **Reaffirmation decisions.** Whether to reaffirm specific debts (typically a car loan the debtor wants to keep) is a court-required analysis. Reaffirming a debt the debtor cannot actually afford reproduces the original problem.
- **Exemption planning.** Federal-vs-state choice, timing of asset acquisition or transfer, homestead-exemption strategies. Attorney work in the debtor's specific state.
- **Dischargeability disputes.** When a creditor challenges the dischargeability of a specific debt (fraud, willful and malicious injury, etc.), defending the dispute requires counsel.
- **Post-discharge issues.** A client whose discharged debt is still being collected on, or whose credit report still shows discharged debts as active, may need legal help to enforce the discharge.

The coach's posture: educate on structure, surface diagnostic signals, route on filing decisions, support post-discharge rebuilding.

## Practical Application

Counseling moments:

- **Bringing it up.** Many clients won't surface bankruptcy themselves because of stigma. The coach's job is to put it on the table as an option alongside reduction strategies — not recommending it, but ensuring the client knows it exists and has the frame to evaluate it.
- **Evaluating fit.** Walk through the diagnostic signals above. Sometimes the honest assessment surfaces that the client has more runway than they thought; sometimes it surfaces that reduction strategies are chasing a lost cause.
- **Making the referral.** The coach doesn't file bankruptcy; a bankruptcy attorney does. Many states have legal-aid programs for low-income debtors; the federal courts maintain a directory of bankruptcy attorneys.
- **Pre-filing conduct.** A client who has decided to file should work with their attorney on pre-filing conduct; a client who hasn't yet retained an attorney should at least not make things worse.
- **Credit counseling requirement.** BAPCPA requires debtors to complete a credit counseling course from an approved nonprofit agency within 6 months before filing, and a financial management course before discharge is granted. The attorney directs the client to compliant providers.
- **Post-discharge rebuilding.** Immediately applicable; don't wait. Secured cards, on-time payments on surviving obligations, responsible use of any post-discharge credit.

For an AI coach, bankruptcy content needs careful handling. The coach can:

- **Educate on structure.** Chapter 7 vs Chapter 13, automatic stay, dischargeability categories, exemption landscape, tax treatment under §108.
- **Surface diagnostic signals.** When the client's data shows patterns that signal bankruptcy might be the right tool — debt-to-income out of range, payoff timelines beyond reasonable horizons, multiple judgments — the coach should name it.
- **Never prepare the filing.** Specialist legal work. Route to bankruptcy attorneys, legal aid, court-based resources.
- **Support the rebuilding.** Post-discharge, track the Rebuild playbook, surface secured-card and credit-builder-loan opportunities, monitor the credit report for errors or items that should have been discharged but still appear.

Resist the mirror temptation — being too cautious about mentioning bankruptcy because of stigma. Clients who need the tool often don't get it because no one they trust raised it as an option.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Waiting too long to file.** A client who drains retirement accounts and home equity trying to pay debts before filing often ends up in the same bankruptcy with fewer exemption-protected assets. ERISA's broad protection of retirement accounts makes pre-filing retirement-account withdrawals particularly costly.
- **Filing the wrong chapter.** Chapter 7 when Chapter 13 would have protected important property, or Chapter 13 when the client cannot realistically complete the plan.
- **Incurring new debt before filing.** Debts incurred shortly before filing (especially cash advances, luxury purchases, or new credit cards) can be challenged as non-dischargeable.
- **Preferential payments to certain creditors pre-filing.** Paying back family or favored creditors shortly before filing can be clawed back by the trustee.
- **Not listing all debts or assets.** The petition requires complete disclosure. Omissions can lead to discharge being denied or revoked.
- **Reaffirming a debt the debtor cannot afford.** Re-obligates the debtor to a debt that was about to be discharged.
- **Assuming all debts are discharged.** Student loans, recent taxes, support obligations, and certain other categories usually survive bankruptcy.
- **Not completing Chapter 13 plan payments.** Failed Chapter 13 results in dismissal without discharge; the debtor keeps the debts and has lost the time and money invested.
- **Using bankruptcy as a first-line tool without addressing underlying behavior.** A client who discharges debt without fixing the spending pattern typically rebuilds the same problem.
- **Not starting Rebuild immediately post-discharge.** Credit recovery is fastest when active rebuilding starts right away.
