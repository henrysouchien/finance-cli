# Balance Sheet and Net Worth Statement

A balance sheet is a point-in-time list of what a household owns and owes. Net
worth is assets minus liabilities. Together, they show the accumulated position
that the cash-flow statement cannot show by itself.

This topic supports planning intake and monitoring. It does not value private
businesses, real estate, collectibles, pensions, legal claims, or tax basis with
specialist precision.

## Structure

Group assets by planning use:

- liquid cash: checking, savings, money market, short-term cash equivalents;
- short-horizon savings: goal-specific cash or near-cash reserves;
- investment assets: taxable brokerage, retirement accounts, HSA investments,
  and other long-horizon accounts;
- use assets: primary residence, vehicles, household property;
- business or other complex assets, when the user identifies them.

Group liabilities by repayment behavior:

- credit cards and other revolving debt;
- installment loans such as auto, student, personal, or medical debt;
- mortgage or home-equity debt;
- tax debt, collections, judgments, or other specialist territory;
- business or personally guaranteed obligations.

The statement should label data quality. Linked-account balances, manual values,
estimated property values, and user-stated debts should not look equally
verified.

## Net Worth Use

Net worth answers different questions than cash flow:

- Is the household building assets or drawing them down over time?
- Is wealth concentrated in illiquid assets?
- Are liabilities shrinking or growing?
- Does apparent wealth hide a cash-reserve problem?
- Are investment or retirement balances large enough to create handoff needs?

High net worth does not guarantee short-term safety. A user can own a home and
retirement accounts while still being unable to handle a $1,000 emergency.
Likewise, negative net worth does not mean every plan fails; a user with strong
cash flow can improve net worth quickly.

## Planning Checks

Use the balance sheet with sibling topics:

- `general_principles.cash-flow-statement` for surplus or deficit;
- `general_principles.personal-financial-ratios.liquidity` for emergency runway;
- `general_principles.personal-financial-ratios.debt-to-income` for debt load;
- `general_principles.financial-planning-snapshot` for cross-domain triage.

Important flags:

- low liquid assets compared with monthly expenses;
- high-interest debt alongside idle cash;
- accounts whose ownership, beneficiary, tax treatment, or restrictions are
  unclear;
- manual balances that have not been updated recently;
- asset or debt categories requiring attorney, CPA, fiduciary, insurance, or
  plan-provider review.

## Practical Application

For `coach_financial_plan_intake`, the balance sheet should become a concise
section of the Financial Planning Snapshot:

- liquid cash;
- investment and retirement balances;
- debt totals and high-interest debt;
- net worth trend when available;
- confidence notes and stale/manual balance warnings;
- planning implications.

Avoid false precision. A snapshot can say "home value is user-estimated and not
used for transfer or debt decisions" instead of pretending the estimate is an
appraisal.

## Common Pitfalls

- Treating net worth as the only planning score.
- Counting illiquid assets as emergency reserves.
- Mixing user-estimated and institution-reported values without labels.
- Ignoring account ownership, beneficiary, tax, or withdrawal restrictions.
- Updating the spending plan but never refreshing the balance sheet.
