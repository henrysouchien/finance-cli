# Mortgage Types

Mortgage types are the vocabulary layer for homebuying readiness. The coach should help the user understand the menu and the questions to ask, but should not choose a product or certify eligibility.

The major dimensions are who backs the loan, how the rate behaves, and how the loan amortizes.

## Who Backs or Originates the Loan

**Conventional mortgage.** A private lender originates the loan without direct federal insurance or guarantee. Conforming conventional loans follow Fannie Mae/Freddie Mac guidelines and loan limits; non-conforming loans include jumbo or otherwise non-standard loans. Conventional loans with less than 20% down usually involve PMI.

**FHA-insured mortgage.** A private lender originates the loan and FHA insures it. HUD describes FHA loans as offering low down payments, low closing costs, and easier credit qualifying; FHA public materials state that down payment can be as low as 3.5% of purchase price for qualifying borrowers. FHA mortgage insurance has different duration rules from conventional PMI.

**VA-backed loan.** A private lender originates the loan and VA guarantees part of it for eligible veterans, service members, and certain survivors. VA public materials emphasize no VA-required down payment in many cases and no private mortgage insurance, while lenders still underwrite credit, income, and property requirements.

**USDA Rural Development loan.** USDA programs support eligible rural homebuyers. The Guaranteed Loan Program provides a guarantee to approved lenders and can allow no-money-down financing for qualifying rural borrowers. Direct loans support low- and very-low-income applicants in eligible rural areas and can include payment assistance.

## Rate Structure

**Fixed-rate mortgage.** The principal-and-interest payment is fixed for the loan term. Taxes, insurance, HOA, and mortgage insurance can still change. Fixed rate generally fits users who plan to stay longer and value payment predictability.

**Adjustable-rate mortgage.** An ARM has an initial fixed period and then adjusts based on an index plus a margin, subject to caps. An ARM can fit a short expected tenure or a user who can absorb reset risk, but it is dangerous when the user cannot afford the payment at reset or lacks a credible exit plan.

## Amortization

Mortgage amortization means the scheduled payment is calculated so the loan pays down over the term. Early payments are interest-heavy; later payments are principal-heavy. Extra principal payments reduce future interest and shorten payoff time, but they are separate from the homebuying-readiness decision unless the user is comparing terms.

Shorter terms, such as 15 years, generally carry higher payments and lower total interest than 30-year terms. The readiness question is whether the higher payment leaves enough margin for reserves and the rest of the spending plan.

## Offer Comparison Vocabulary

Key comparison fields:

- interest rate;
- APR;
- loan term;
- points;
- lender fees;
- third-party closing costs;
- rate-lock terms;
- mortgage insurance structure;
- prepayment penalty or balloon features;
- total cash to close;
- projected monthly payment.

The Loan Estimate and Closing Disclosure are standardized mortgage-disclosure documents. `general_principles.home-buying-process` covers when the user sees them in the transaction.

## Hand-Off Discipline

This is a vocabulary topic. The coach may define terms and help the user form questions. The coach must hand off product selection.

- Do not tell the user they qualify for FHA, VA, USDA, conventional, jumbo, or any other product.
- Do not recommend a specific product as the best mortgage for the user.
- Do not quote current program limits, income thresholds, funding-fee tables, guarantee fees, or loan limits unless a current official source is checked in the same engagement.
- Do not compare live lender offers in v0.1 except at a high-level checklist; detailed Loan Estimate comparison is a future surface.
- Route local, program, or eligibility questions to a lender, program administrator, or `referrals.hud-approved-housing-counselor`.

## Common Pitfalls

- Treating "mortgage" as one product.
- Taking an ARM without a realistic reset or exit plan.
- Comparing rates while ignoring APR and fees.
- Ignoring mortgage-insurance duration.
- Missing VA or USDA as possible questions to ask when the user may be eligible.
- Assuming FHA is automatically better than low-down-payment conventional, or vice versa.
- Paying points without expecting to hold the loan long enough to recover the cost.
