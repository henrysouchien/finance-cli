# Short-Term Financing

Short-term financing is defined by timing, not product type: any borrowing arrangement intended to bridge a small gap (typically $100 to a few thousand dollars) over days or weeks until expected income arrives. The contexts are concrete and urgent — a car repair to keep getting to work, a rent payment to avoid eviction, a medical copay, a utility bill at shutoff threshold.

The bind: the client facing the gap usually has the worst access to good options. Mainstream unsecured credit (personal loans, available cash-advance room on a non-maxed credit card, HELOC) isn't reachable for everyone, and the products explicitly designed to fill the gap — payday loans, auto-title loans, rent-to-own, pawnshop lending — price at levels that quickly do more damage than the gap itself. The coach's value here is naming the ladder honestly: the top of the ladder is materially better than the bottom, and most clients don't know the top exists.

This topic is **`scope: framing_only`** — the coach educates, surfaces options, and routes to specialists. The coach does not arrange the debt management plan, negotiate with creditors directly on the client's behalf, or recommend a specific named lender. See the Hand-Off Discipline section below.

## Why the Ladder Matters: Cycle Dynamics

The lower rungs of the ladder matter disproportionately because the predatory tier is engineered to be sticky. Payday loans in particular run on rollover by design: arrival of the next paycheck doesn't fill the original cash-flow gap — and now a piece of that paycheck is already promised to the lender — so the borrower pays another fee and extends. The CFPB's payday-market research has repeatedly found that the bulk of industry revenue comes from borrowers who re-borrow several times within a year, not from one-and-done borrowers. The "borrow $300 once" framing collapses on contact with how the cycle actually plays out.

Naming this dynamic up front changes how the coach frames the ladder: the goal isn't to find the cheapest single-use option, it's to avoid the products whose business model assumes the user gets stuck.

## Lower-Cost Options (Top of the Ladder)

These are the options to exhaust before considering the predatory tier. Most clients don't know they exist or assume they don't qualify.

### Small-Dollar Personal Loans at Banks and Credit Unions

Loan products in the $500–$2,500 range with installment terms of 3–12 months, priced at single-digit or low-double-digit APRs rather than the triple-digit APRs of payday lending. Availability is institution-specific — not every bank carries one — but most major credit unions and many community banks do.

Qualifying friction: lenders generally want to see established credit history plus a prior relationship with the institution. Thin-file or no-file clients often won't get approved on the first ask. For clients who clear the bar, this is the right first stop.

### Payday Alternative Loans (PALs) at Federal Credit Unions

A specifically regulated product under NCUA's PAL rule (12 CFR 701.21). Federal credit unions may offer small-dollar short-term loans ($200–$2,000 depending on tier) at an interest rate up to 28% (1,000 basis points above the Federal Credit Union loan ceiling), plus a separate cap on application fees. Terms run 1–12 months.

Membership is the gate. Federal credit union membership criteria are usually geographic, employer-based, or affinity-based and broadly inclusive, and a client can often join on the spot. The friction of opening membership is small relative to the cost savings versus the next tier.

Not every federal credit union actually offers PALs — the rule is permissive, not mandatory. Worth calling local options to confirm before sending the client in.

### Employer-Based Programs

Some employers offer short-term loan programs, hardship grants, or partnership-based early-wage-access products as a benefit. These are typically not advertised at the level of 401(k) or health insurance, and many workers don't know they exist. One question to HR closes the information gap.

Retirement-plan loans are the employer-channel option that deserves the most careful framing. The client borrows against their own future retirement balance and repays through payroll deductions; the interest paid returns to the client's own account. The risk: separation from the employer usually accelerates the unpaid balance, which becomes a plan loan offset. The offset is treated as taxable income unless the borrower rolls it over into another retirement plan or IRA by the federal tax return due date (including extensions); borrowers under 59½ may also owe a 10% additional early-distribution tax unless an exception applies. Workable for many clients; the tax mechanics need to be understood before signing.

### Negotiating with Creditors Directly

Often the highest-leverage move on the list, and it costs a phone call. The pattern: utilities will set up a payment arrangement rather than absorb the cost of a disconnect-and-restore cycle; medical providers frequently carry hardship discounts or interest-free installment terms that are only available if the patient asks for them; landlords usually prefer a partial payment plus a short extension to the cost and time of initiating eviction.

A coaching pass through "who do you owe, and which of those will work with you" before any borrowing recommendation often eliminates or shrinks the cash need to a level the other tiers don't need to address.

### Family and Peer Lending

Frequently the cheapest source of funds. Frequently the most complicated. The standing recommendation when this is the path: even with family, document the terms in writing (amount, repayment schedule, whether interest applies). Informal loans that go unpaid damage relationships in ways that dwarf the dollar impact, and the documentation discipline protects both sides.

### Credit-Card Cash Advances

Expensive — see `general_principles.credit-cards` for the fee-plus-no-grace-period structure — but quantitatively cheaper than anything in the predatory tier. A typical $500 cash advance at a 5% fee plus 30 days of 26% APR costs roughly $35, or about 7% of the advance. The equivalent in payday-loan pricing is $75–$150 every two weeks. Cash advances are a bad option that is less bad than what comes next.

## The Predatory Tier

Products specifically engineered to capture clients who've exhausted (or never had) the lower-cost options. High effective rates wrapped in non-APR fee disclosures; structures built around rollover.

### Payday Loans

A small, short-term loan ($100–$500 typical) due in full on the borrower's next payday. The cost is framed as a fee per amount borrowed ($15–$30 per $100, per two-week term) rather than as an interest rate. Annualized, that fee structure corresponds to **300–500%+ APR equivalents**.

The structural problem is the one named above: the next paycheck rarely closes the gap, so the loan rolls. The federal regulatory response — the CFPB's Payday, Vehicle Title, and Certain High-Cost Installment Loans rule (12 CFR Part 1041) — was originally designed to address the rollover dynamic, but its 2017 final-rule mandatory underwriting / ability-to-repay provisions were revoked in 2020. What survived is the payment-transfer side: a prohibition on lenders attempting further withdrawals from a borrower's account after two consecutive failed attempts, plus required disclosures and notices, all operative March 30, 2025. The CFPB has signaled it will not prioritize enforcement penalties under those surviving provisions. Practically: the federal cap on rollover behavior never landed. The protection picture is now primarily state-level — many states impose cooling-off periods between loans, rollover caps, or installment-conversion options once a borrower has hit a certain rollover count.

Coaching frame: a client in a payday-loan cycle is in a higher-priority intervention than whatever the presenting money issue is. Each rollover is more fees out the door. The standing referral is a nonprofit credit counseling agency (NFCC member or FCAA member — see referrals); a federal credit union PAL is the typical product-side replacement.

### Auto-Title Loans

A 30-day-typical loan collateralized by the title to the borrower's vehicle. The borrower keeps possession and keeps driving; the lender holds the title and can repossess on default. Effective APRs commonly land in the 100–300% range, with the same rollover dynamics as payday loans.

The cascade failure mode is what makes auto-title loans particularly destructive: losing the vehicle usually means losing access to the job that pays the bills, which then collapses the borrower's ability to recover. The "short-term" framing obscures how much downstream stability is being collateralized.

### Rent-to-Own

Not a loan in form but functionally similar. A weekly or monthly payment arrangement on a consumer good (furniture, appliance, electronics) where title transfers at the end of the payment stream. Total paid is typically 2–3× the retail price of the item if the client completes the full schedule; the client holds no equity until the final payment; missed payments trigger repossession with no refund of payments already made.

Almost always cheaper to save and buy the item used, or to use a major-retailer layaway program (which preserves refundability). For clients already mid-stream on a rent-to-own contract, the sunk-cost math sometimes favors completing the schedule rather than walking away, but the lesson should inform the next purchase, not become a justification.

### Pawnshop Lending

A loan secured by a personal item (jewelry, electronics, tools) the pawnshop holds for the term. The loan amount is usually 25–60% of the item's resale value; the term is commonly 30 days with a renewal option; fees run 10–25% per month.

Pawnshop lending has one structural advantage over payday or title loans: the downside is capped at losing the pawned item. There's no residual debt if the client can't repay — the item is forfeited, the loan is closed. For a one-time genuine bridge where the client has a specific item they could part with if things went wrong, pawnshop lending can be a less-bad choice than the rollover products. For recurring use, the math gets ugly fast.

## The Middle Ground (Alternative Financial Services)

Products that sit between the lower-cost and predatory tiers. Some are useful; some are predatory wrapped in better marketing.

### Secured Credit Cards and Credit-Builder Loans

Covered in `general_principles.building-credit`. Neither is short-term liquidity per se: secured credit cards require a deposit before the matching credit limit becomes usable, and credit-builder loans hold the borrowed amount in savings until the term completes. The relevance here is infrastructure — establishing one of these *before* the next short-term gap creates access to the standard credit-card grace period (often the best short-bridge option for a few-weeks gap once the card is established).

### Buy Now, Pay Later (BNPL)

Short-term installment plans offered at checkout by providers like Affirm, Klarna, Afterpay, and various retailer-branded products. Typical structure: four payments over six weeks at 0% interest if paid on schedule, with late fees if missed. Some longer-term BNPL products (3–12 months) carry interest disclosed under TILA.

Three issues the coach should name explicitly:

- **Reporting practice, shifting unevenly.** Historically, most BNPL providers reported only missed payments to the credit bureaus — full downside credit damage, no on-time-payment credit-building benefit. The pattern is shifting unevenly: Affirm now reports all pay-over-time loans to Experian and TransUnion; Sezzle Up reports for opted-in users; most standard BNPL plans still do not automatically furnish on-time data. Even where reporting exists, BNPL data may appear on credit files without immediately influencing traditional credit-score models, which haven't fully integrated the data class. The client should not assume on-time BNPL activity is building their traditional score unless the specific provider has confirmed reporting AND the relevant scoring model factors that data.
- **Stacking risk.** BNPL makes it easy to run several concurrent payment streams without mentally accounting for the total monthly cash flow committed. A client with five active BNPL plans for different purchases is servicing $300–$500 per month of obligations they aren't tracking.
- **Return complications.** Returns on BNPL-financed purchases can leave the BNPL service still collecting payments while the retailer processes the refund separately, producing temporary double-charging that the client has to chase.

Occasional use that pays on schedule is essentially fine. BNPL functioning as ongoing payroll smoothing is a different signal — the visible symptom of an underlying cash-flow shortfall rather than a useful response to one.

### Earned-Wage Access / Early-Wage Access

Products that let workers pull earned-but-unpaid wages before the scheduled payday, either through an employer partnership or a direct-to-consumer app. Pricing is typically a small flat fee per advance, a subscription fee, or a "voluntary tip" (often defaulting to a suggested amount that operates as an effective fee).

Genuinely cheaper than payday lending per use. Still adds up — a client who advances every pay period and tips the default amount is paying meaningfully for cash-flow timing that a smoothed-out spending plan would eliminate. EWA can help in a one-off crunch; as a standing pattern, it's a signal that `general_principles.spending-plan` and `general_principles.cash-flow-statement` need attention.

## Order-of-Operations in a Crisis

The sequence a coach typically walks with a client mid-pinch:

1. **Re-examine the need.** Is the cash genuinely needed now, or is the urgency partially imagined? Can the underlying obligation be deferred or negotiated?
2. **Existing earmarked savings.** Any revolving sub-account (see `general_principles.banking-basics`) that exists for exactly this kind of expense? Drawing it down is usually cheaper than any borrowing.
3. **Direct creditor negotiation.** Utility payment plan, medical hardship discount, landlord conversation. Often shrinks or eliminates the cash need entirely.
4. **Small-dollar loan at a bank or credit union.** First external borrowing option. If the client isn't already a credit union member, joining is frequently possible the same day.
5. **Employer-based programs.** One HR question — don't assume the program doesn't exist.
6. **Family or peer lending, where available and relationally healthy.** Document terms in writing.
7. **Credit-card cash advance, with full awareness of cost.**
8. **Pawnshop lending, for one-time use with a capped downside.**
9. **Predatory tier (payday, auto-title, rent-to-own)** — only if every option above is exhausted, and even then with an explicit plan to break the cycle at the first opportunity.

Steps 1–3 are the ones the coach most often instinctively skips. They are also the ones most likely to make the borrowing unnecessary.

## Hand-Off Discipline

This topic is `framing_only`. The coach surfaces the ladder, names the dynamics, and walks the order-of-operations. The coach does **not**:

- **Negotiate with creditors on the client's behalf.** The coach can prep the client for the conversation (script, what to ask for, what fallback to accept), but the call is the client's.
- **Recommend a specific named lender or product.** Surfacing categories ("a small-dollar loan from your credit union," "ask HR about employer programs") is in scope. Naming a specific lender is not — both because lender quality varies locally and because endorsement creates a different relationship.
- **Arrange a debt management plan.** Clients in an active payday-loan cycle or struggling to keep up with multiple short-term obligations should be referred to a **nonprofit credit counseling agency** (NFCC member or FCAA member — see `referrals.nfcc` and `referrals.fcaa-member-agency-finder`). The DMP itself, including the negotiated rate reductions with creditors, is the agency's work.
- **Interpret state payday-lending law.** Many states impose cooling-off periods, rollover caps, or installment-conversion rules; the specifics vary by state and change. The coach surfaces that state-specific protections may exist and refers the client to the state's department of financial regulation or attorney general for current rules.
- **Recommend bankruptcy as a tool here.** Bankruptcy is a separate domain (see `general_principles.bankruptcy`) with its own referral discipline to a bankruptcy attorney.

## Practical Application

Short-term financing comes up in coaching at four distinct moments:

- **Crisis intake.** Client walks in mid-crisis needing cash. Work through steps 1–3 of the order-of-operations before any lending recommendation. The better solution often isn't a loan.
- **Recurring short-term borrowing pattern.** Multiple payday-loan or BNPL transactions inside a 12-month window are usually surface signs of something different: an ongoing structural cash-flow shortfall. The coaching work routes back to the budget and inflow/outflow picture (`general_principles.cash-flow-statement`, `general_principles.spending-plan`) rather than to shopping for a less expensive borrowing product.
- **Active payday-loan cycle.** This is an escalation case. Each new biweekly term layers more fees onto the original principal, and inertia tends to extend the cycle further. Standing tools: nonprofit credit-counseling referral (NFCC or FCAA member) and a search for an available federal credit union PAL the client could roll into.
- **Building resilience preemptively.** Outside of an active crisis, the work is preventive — install the infrastructure that turns the next short-term gap into a non-event. That means a funded liquid reserve (see `general_principles.personal-financial-ratios.liquidity`), credit union membership opened before it's needed, and a non-maxed credit card reserved for genuine emergencies rather than for discretionary spending.

For an AI coach, the underlying signal pattern is unusually readable from transaction data. Recurring BNPL merchant charges, payday-loan repayment lines (often labeled distinctively in the merchant string), retirement-plan loan deductions on a pay statement, pawnshop transactions — all detectable. The coach can raise the pattern proactively rather than waiting for the user to bring it up: a prompt like "I'm seeing four separate BNPL providers active over the past quarter — want to walk through what the aggregate cash-flow picture looks like?" surfaces the issue without requiring the client to self-diagnose. Where the user has linked a credit union or bank, the coach can also prompt at category level — "before the next time you'd reach for a payday loan, it's worth asking whether your credit union offers a PAL" — without naming a specific product or endorsing a specific institution's offering (per Hand-Off Discipline above).

One framing caution: the coach should be careful about how it lists predatory-tier options. Even an informational "here are payday lenders near you" can look like endorsement. The preferred framing is the ladder — "if you need short-term cash, here is the order of options, with referrals to credit-union PALs and nonprofit credit counseling."

## Common Pitfalls

- **Treating payday loans as a one-time tool.** The product is designed to roll, and the majority of revenue comes from borrowers who do. Any "just this once" use that doesn't actually pay in full on the first due date is most likely the start of a cycle.
- **Losing the vehicle to an auto-title default.** Almost always cascades into job loss. Exhaust every other option first; if an auto-title loan happens despite the warning, plan aggressively for the first scheduled repayment to avoid the rollover.
- **Paying 2–3× retail through rent-to-own.** The total-paid math is obscured at signing by the focus on the weekly or monthly payment. Walk through it explicitly before the client commits.
- **Stacking BNPL plans without aggregate tracking.** Five active plans for unrelated purchases can quietly consume several hundred dollars of monthly cash flow that the client doesn't realize is committed. Each BNPL agreement belongs on the budget as an explicit line.
- **Relying on earned-wage-access as a standing pattern.** Cheap per use, expensive over a year. Frequent use is a signal to fix the cash flow, not a substitute for fixing it.
- **Skipping the HR question.** Employer-side short-term loan programs, hardship grant funds, and EWA partnerships exist at many employers but are rarely well-publicized. A single conversation often surfaces an option the client didn't know about.
- **Not joining a credit union pre-emptively.** Credit unions hold the best products in this category, but membership takes minutes that a client in crisis doesn't have. Joining before the next crisis makes the option actually available when needed.
