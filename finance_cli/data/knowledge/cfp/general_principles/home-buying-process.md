# Home Buying Process

The home-buying process has four stages: get ready, search and offer, negotiate and finance, then close. This topic gives the vocabulary and order of operations a client needs before they sign documents or spend money on an offer.

For `coach_homebuying_readiness` v0.1, only the readiness and preapproval-preparation portions are in direct scope. Transaction-stage tasks such as contract negotiation, inspection interpretation, Loan Estimate comparison, and Closing Disclosure review are referral or future-skill territory.

## Stage 1: Get Ready

The readiness stage happens before shopping.

- Save for down payment, closing costs, moving costs, immediate-post-close costs, and post-close reserve.
- Review all three credit reports and fix errors before a hard mortgage inquiry.
- Run the affordability calculation in `general_principles.home-affordability`.
- Decide whether the user is only exploring, ready for informal prequalification, or ready for formal preapproval.
- Clarify needs vs. wants, location priorities, home type, and condition tolerance.

Prequalification and preapproval are not the same. Prequalification is an informal estimate based on limited information. Preapproval is stronger because it usually involves verified documents and a hard credit pull. A user should not treat a prequalification number as proof that a purchase is prudent.

## Stage 2: Search and Offer

The user looks at homes, chooses whether to work with a buyer's agent, and submits a written offer. The offer usually includes price, contingencies, earnest money, timeline, and included items.

Earnest money is not the same as down payment. Earnest money is a deposit accompanying the offer and is later applied to closing if the transaction completes. It may be at risk if the buyer exits outside valid contingencies.

## Stage 3: Negotiate, Finance, and Do Due Diligence

After an accepted offer, the buyer typically moves through inspection, appraisal, purchase-and-sale agreement, formal mortgage application, Loan Estimate review, rate-lock decision, and repair or credit negotiations.

The inspection and appraisal do different jobs. The inspection evaluates condition; the appraisal supports lender valuation. A low appraisal or serious inspection issue can change the cash required, trigger renegotiation, or lead the buyer to walk away if a contingency permits it.

The Loan Estimate is a standardized mortgage disclosure. The user's job is to compare loan terms, monthly payment, cash-to-close, lender fees, third-party costs, mortgage insurance, rate-lock terms, and any risky features such as prepayment penalties.

## Stage 4: Prepare to Close and Close

Before closing, the buyer obtains homeowners insurance, reviews the Closing Disclosure, completes the final walk-through, and signs the closing documents if the terms match expectations.

The Closing Disclosure is the final disclosure of loan terms, projected payments, closing costs, and cash to close. CFPB guidance emphasizes using the required review window to ask questions and resolve differences from the Loan Estimate before signing.

## Practical Application

For readiness coaching:

- Keep the user in Stage 1 until cash, reserves, credit, and DTI are coherent.
- Define whether the next human step is a HUD-approved housing counselor, lender prequalification, lender preapproval, or waiting.
- Build a preapproval checklist: income documents, bank statements, tax records if self-employed, ID, debt list, credit report review, down-payment source documentation, and questions for lenders.
- Surface transaction vocabulary early enough that the user recognizes it later.
- Treat active contract, inspection, appraisal, or closing questions as referral or future-skill topics.

## Hand-Off Discipline

The coach may educate on process sequence, vocabulary, readiness, and checklist preparation. The coach must hand off when the user is making binding transaction decisions.

- Do not draft, interpret, or negotiate purchase contracts; route legal questions to an attorney or qualified real estate professional.
- Do not choose a lender, agent, inspector, appraiser, title company, or insurer for the user.
- Do not certify program eligibility or down-payment-assistance eligibility; route to the program administrator or `referrals.hud-approved-housing-counselor`.
- Do not decide whether to waive contingencies.
- Do not perform a legal tolerance analysis of a Loan Estimate or Closing Disclosure in v0.1; explain the documents and route detailed disputes to the lender, CFPB resources, HUD counselor, attorney, or relevant regulator.

## Common Pitfalls

- Starting house tours before readiness math is complete.
- Making offers with only prequalification when the market expects preapproval.
- Waiving inspection or appraisal contingencies without understanding the risk.
- Treating earnest money as if it were extra down-payment money.
- Getting new credit between preapproval and closing.
- Accepting the first Loan Estimate without comparison.
- Not comparing the Closing Disclosure against the Loan Estimate.
- Skipping the final walk-through.
