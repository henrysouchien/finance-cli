# Cash Flow Statement

A cash flow statement is a historical summary of a household's income and expenses over a defined window — typically a month. It answers one diagnostic question: did the household live within its means during that window? The arithmetic is total income minus total expenses; the result is either a surplus (income exceeded expenses) or a deficit (expenses exceeded income). The surplus or deficit is the starting input for next month's spending plan.

A cash flow statement is distinct from a net worth statement. Cash flow tracks movement; net worth tracks accumulated value. A household can post consistent monthly surpluses while carrying negative net worth from large outstanding debts. A retiree drawing down savings may run a deficit on paper while remaining solvent. Both statements together give the full financial picture; neither is sufficient alone.

## Key Concepts

**Income side.** Use **net pay** (after-tax take-home) on a personal cash flow statement, since that is the cash the household actually directs. Gross income is reserved for ratios such as debt-to-income (the gross-vs-net distinction is a common pitfall in untrained budgeting). For irregular earners, average across a recent window — three months for variable W-2 work; twelve months for seasonal self-employment.

**Expense types.** Three categories, each behaving differently:

- **Fixed expenses** — predictable schedule and amount: rent or mortgage, car payment, insurance premiums billed monthly, regular savings contributions.
- **Variable expenses** — fluctuate month to month: groceries, utilities, gas, clothing, medical bills, childcare, entertainment. Variable does not mean optional, but it is where tactical month-to-month adjustments can move the number.
- **Periodic expenses** — predictable but non-monthly: annual property taxes, quarterly insurance premiums, vehicle registration, holiday spending, vacations. The standard fix is to divide the annual cost by 12 and reserve that amount each month, so the bill does not arrive as a shock.

**Surplus and deficit.** Total income minus total expenses. A positive result is a surplus; a negative result is a deficit. The number itself is the beginning of the analysis, not the end — what the categories reveal about priorities is where behavior change starts.

## Constructing a Cash Flow Statement

Standard 4-step workflow:

1. **Collect income.** Sum net pay from each earner's pay stubs plus after-tax investment income.
2. **Collect monthly expenses.** Categorize the last full month of bank and card transactions into fixed and variable buckets. Build from actual records, never from memory — humans systematically underestimate variable expenses.
3. **Annualize and smooth periodic expenses.** Sum known periodic costs annually and divide by 12 for the monthly reserve.
4. **Compute the surplus or deficit.** Total income minus total expenses; report unambiguously.

### Worked example

A two-earner family of four with combined monthly net income of $6,500 (wages $5,835 after taxes plus $665 after-tax investment income) and total monthly expenses of $6,100 (rent $2,400, utilities $150, groceries $400, transportation $490, insurance $430, loan and credit payments $800, entertainment and dining $330, savings contributions $350, health expenses $300, miscellaneous $450) produces a monthly surplus of $400 — the pool available for additional savings, accelerated debt paydown, or absorbing a periodic expense.

## Cash Flow Timing

Even a statement showing a healthy surplus can mask a timing problem. If rent is due on the 1st but the paycheck lands on the 15th, the household may overdraft despite positive monthly net flow. When the cash-flow math looks fine but the client reports missed or late bills, the next diagnostic is a calendar — a month laid out day by day with income and expense events plotted where they actually hit. A spreadsheet, a notebook, or a wall calendar all work; the goal is to reveal the timing mismatch the aggregate statement hides.

## Practical Application

For an AI coach, a cash flow statement is where ambient transaction data becomes useful. Categorized transactions over the last 30 days produce a draft statement automatically — no intake form needed. The coaching work is reviewing it with the client: flagging surprising categories, confirming that a surplus is real or that a deficit is narrowing, and using the numbers to ground the next conversation. Never show a computed surplus without showing the categories that produced it — otherwise the number feels like magic and the client cannot act on it.

The cash flow statement is the first shared artifact of most coaching sessions. Abstract client concerns ("I can't save anything") become concrete numbers. The document itself is not the insight; the insight comes from reviewing it with the client and asking which categories feel right, which feel wrong, and which reveal something the client did not know.

When income drops and cash flow turns negative, surfacing safety-net income sources is part of the diagnostic. Federal and state programs (SNAP, TANF, Medicaid, Section 8 housing, LIHEAP, WIC) stabilize households during income disruptions; eligibility, benefit amounts, and application mechanics vary by state.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Building from memory instead of records.** Always source from actual transaction data; memory underestimates variable expenses.
- **Ignoring periodic expenses.** Treating the twelve-month ledger as twelve identical months produces a budget that breaks the first time property tax lands.
- **Confusing gross with net.** Gross income belongs in DTI and tax comparisons; net belongs in budgeting because it is what the household actually controls.
- **Treating one month as representative.** Single-month data is noisy — a moving three-month average is more useful for decision-making, especially for variable-income households.
- **Stopping at the math.** The surplus or deficit number is the start of the conversation, not the end.
