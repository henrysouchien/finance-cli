# Credit Cards

Credit cards occupy a strange position in consumer finance. Used well, they are almost costless — 25+ days of free borrowing every cycle, fraud protection stronger than cash or debit, rewards, travel benefits, a credit-building mechanism. Used poorly, they are the most common source of consumer debt in the US, with average APRs in the 20%+ range that can take decades to pay off at minimum payments.

The difference is not product choice. A single card used with the right habits outperforms an elaborate multi-card strategy used sloppily. The counselor's job is rarely to recommend a specific card; it is to install the habits that keep the card on the "tool" side of the ledger.

Credit cards are the dominant revolving-credit product. See `general_principles.consumer-credit-types` for the broader installment/revolving/service taxonomy and amortization mechanics.

## What a Credit Card Actually Is

A credit card is a revolving-credit product. The issuer (a bank, credit union, or store financing arm, usually operating under a network brand like Visa, Mastercard, American Express, or Discover) extends a **credit limit** — the maximum the cardholder can borrow at any time. Each purchase draws against that limit; each payment restores it. As long as the account is in good standing, the arrangement can continue indefinitely.

Unlike a **debit card**, which draws from the cardholder's own bank account in real time, a credit card draws from the issuer's money and creates an obligation to repay. This simple distinction has large downstream consequences: dispute rights under the Fair Credit Billing Act are stronger for credit than debit; fraud liability is capped lower for credit; the short-term loan structure creates the grace-period mechanic; and the open obligation creates the possibility of interest accrual.

## Essential Terminology

Six terms come up in every statement:

- **APR (Annual Percentage Rate).** Yearly interest rate that applies to balances carried month to month. A card may have a single APR for all activity or different APRs for purchases, balance transfers, and cash advances. The APR is annual but the math runs daily — each day the unpaid balance generates interest at 1/365 of the APR. Typical credit-card APR range: 15–26%.
- **Credit limit.** Maximum the cardholder can borrow. Exceeding the limit is usually declined at purchase, though some cards allow over-limit transactions at the issuer's discretion (and may charge a fee).
- **Grace period.** Window after the statement closes during which the cardholder can pay the full statement balance without any interest charge. Typically 21–25 days. The grace period applies only if the previous month's balance was also paid in full; carrying any balance from the prior cycle usually voids the grace period for new purchases until the full balance is paid off.
- **Minimum payment.** Lowest amount the cardholder must pay each cycle to keep the account in good standing. Typically calculated as a small percentage of the balance (1–3%) plus interest and any fees, or a fixed floor amount ($25–$35), whichever is larger. Paying only the minimum is legally sufficient but mathematically punishing (see below).
- **Statement balance.** Balance as of the statement closing date. This is what must be paid to avoid interest. Also the amount usually reported to the credit bureaus for utilization calculation.
- **Available credit.** Credit limit minus current balance and pending transactions.

Statement balance and current balance are usually different. Current balance includes charges made after the statement closed. Paying the current balance in full resets the account to zero; paying the statement balance in full secures the grace period. Either approach avoids interest; the mechanics differ slightly.

## The Fees That Add Up

Every card has some combination of fees. Most are avoidable through behavior or product choice.

- **Annual fee.** Once-a-year charge for holding the card. Common on premium rewards cards ($95–$695+); absent on many standard and no-frills cards. Worth paying only if rewards and benefits the cardholder actually uses exceed the fee.
- **Late payment fee.** Charged when the minimum payment is not received by the due date. Capped under the CARD Act; current caps adjust over time. Beyond the fee, late payments trigger credit-score damage and can cause the issuer to raise the APR (called a **penalty APR**) on the account.
- **Cash advance fee.** Charged when the card is used to obtain cash (ATM withdrawal, OTC bank withdrawal, cash-equivalent transactions). Usually 3–5% of the advance with a minimum floor ($10+). The real cost: cash advances typically carry a higher APR than purchases AND have NO grace period — interest accrues from day one.
- **Balance transfer fee.** Charged when moving a balance from one card to another, typically 3–5%. A balance transfer to a lower-APR card can still net positive, but the fee has to factor into the math: a 0% promotional rate with a 5% transfer fee is effectively a 5% loan for the promotional period.
- **Foreign transaction fee.** Charged on purchases in a foreign currency or through a foreign merchant, typically 1–3% per transaction. Travel-oriented cards often waive this; standard cards often don't.

Less common but worth naming: over-limit fees, returned-payment fees, paper-statement fees, expedited-payment fees, replacement-card fees. Most are avoidable.

## The Minimum Payment Trap

The math of minimum payments is where many clients have their "oh" moment. A $3,000 balance at 18% APR, paid at the minimum each month, takes roughly **12 years** to pay off and costs around **$2,500** in interest — more than 80% of the original balance. Doubling the minimum typically cuts both time and interest by more than half.

The CARD Act requires every credit-card statement to disclose this math directly. Every statement carries a "Minimum Payment Warning" showing:

- How long it will take to pay off the statement balance at the minimum.
- How much total interest the cardholder will pay at the minimum.
- A comparison at a slightly higher payment (usually the payment that would clear the balance in 36 months), showing how much faster and cheaper that path is.

**Counseling move:** when a client is carrying balances, pull out a recent statement and read the Minimum Payment Warning together. Often the first time the client has seen the numbers in that form. The statement doesn't lie; the warning is there because federal law requires the issuer to disclose it.

Why minimum-payment-only produces this result: the minimum is sized to cover interest plus a small portion of principal. Early in repayment, most of the minimum is interest and the balance moves down slowly. As the balance falls, more of the same minimum goes to principal — but by then years have passed. Same amortization logic as in `general_principles.consumer-credit-types`, except at a much higher rate and without a fixed end date.

## The Habits That Separate Tool From Trap

Five behaviors, in order of impact:

1. **Pay the statement balance in full each cycle.** The single most important habit. Eliminates interest entirely, preserves the grace period on future purchases, and keeps the account functioning as a 25-day free loan. Autopay set to "statement balance in full" makes this the default behavior and removes the risk of forgetting.
2. **Keep utilization under 30% (ideally under 10%).** A client who pays in full each cycle but uses 70% of a card's limit right before the statement cuts still looks high-utilization to the bureaus. Mid-cycle payments or spreading spending across cards keeps the reported balance low.
3. **Read the statement.** Every statement. Catch unauthorized charges and errors. The Fair Credit Billing Act gives the cardholder strong dispute rights, but most are time-limited from the date the charge appears on the statement.
4. **Never view the credit limit as spending money.** A $10,000 limit is the maximum the issuer is willing to loan, not money the client has. Plan spending from income and savings; use the card as a payment method, not a funding source.
5. **Understand each fee and APR before applying.** Most card-level regret comes from not reading the terms once. Five minutes with the Schumer box (standardized disclosure at the top of every card agreement, mandated by federal law) answers almost every question.

## Cash Advances: A Special Warning

Cash advances deserve their own line. The fee is typically 3–5%; the APR on cash advances is typically higher than the purchase APR (often 25%+ even on cards with lower purchase rates); and critically, **there is no grace period** — interest begins accruing the moment the cash is withdrawn.

A $500 cash advance at a 5% fee and 26% APR, paid off at the next statement, costs the cardholder roughly $35 ($25 fee + ~$10 interest for the 30-ish days). A $500 purchase paid in full from the same statement costs $0. The cash advance is effectively a 7% fee for a 30-day loan.

Clients who use cash advances are often doing so because they're out of other options — exactly the situation where paying 7% for 30 days is most harmful. Lower-cost alternatives almost always exist.

## When the Card Is Worth Using

The case for using a credit card, despite the risks, is real for clients who have the habits above in place:

- **Fraud protection.** Federal law caps credit-card fraud liability at $50 (most issuers waive entirely). Debit card fraud liability is higher; recovery process is different — fraudulent debit charges can leave the checking account empty until resolved, while fraudulent credit charges are a line on a statement the cardholder hasn't paid yet.
- **Dispute rights (FCBA).** Cardholder may withhold payment on disputed charges while the dispute is investigated. Much stronger than debit or cash.
- **Grace-period float.** Each cycle represents 25+ days during which the cardholder has made purchases but hasn't paid. Working capital the client can deploy elsewhere — for most clients, the practical use is smoothing the cash-flow timing of irregular expenses.
- **Purchase protection, extended warranties, travel insurance.** Many cards bundle these at no extra cost. Often under-used.
- **Rewards.** Cash back (1–2% typical), travel points, sign-up bonuses. For clients paying in full each cycle, rewards are net positive. For clients carrying balances, rewards are dwarfed by interest — the client is paying the issuer more than the issuer is paying them.
- **Credit-building effect.** On-time payments reported monthly contribute to payment-history (35%) and utilization (part of 30%) score components. The card doesn't have to carry a balance to build credit; transactions and on-time full payments do the work.

## Practical Application

Counseling moments around credit cards:

- **During intake**, when the client lists card balances. The total across cards is often a surprise to the client themselves — each card felt manageable in isolation.
- **When considering a new card.** Review annual fee, APR, grace-period rules, and relevant features (rewards, foreign transaction) before the application. Hard-pull every new application, so the choice matters.
- **When balances are carried.** Reading the Minimum Payment Warning from a recent statement is often the motivational move — the 12-year horizon at minimum-only is visceral in a way abstract interest discussions are not. Then decide between snowball, avalanche, or balance-transfer strategies.
- **When utilization is high ahead of a planned financing event.** A client about to apply for a mortgage should be pushing utilization well below 30% on every card — mid-cycle payments, limit increases, or spreading balances across cards.
- **When a cash advance is being considered.** Intervention point. The cost structure is usually misunderstood, and alternatives exist.
- **When rewards are motivating behavior.** Some clients chase sign-up bonuses in ways that net out negative. Rewards only work for cardholders who would have made the spending anyway and pay in full each cycle.

For an AI coach, credit card data is one of the richest longitudinal signals available. The coach can compute reported utilization each month, track statement-balance-to-payment ratio (a proxy for "are they paying in full"), flag cards approaching penalty-APR triggers, and project minimum-payment timelines directly from current balance, APR, and historical payment pattern. When the user asks "should I get this new card?" the coach can evaluate the offer against their current portfolio (annual fee vs. actual rewards usage, hard-inquiry cost given other planned applications, utilization improvement from a higher total limit). This is exactly the kind of personalized, data-grounded analysis hard for a client to do from a statement alone.

Be conservative about specific product recommendations — card offers change constantly, and training data goes stale faster than offers do — but the coach can reliably evaluate the terms of a card the user is already considering.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Carrying a balance "to build credit."** Myth. On-time payments build credit; interest accrual builds the issuer's revenue. Pay in full every cycle.
- **Paying the current balance vs. the statement balance without understanding the difference.** Both avoid interest if done consistently; grace-period mechanics differ slightly. Statement balance is the standard target.
- **Treating rewards as income.** Rewards are a discount on spending. Spending to earn rewards is a losing trade.
- **Chasing sign-up bonuses while carrying balances.** The bonus is ~$200–$500; interest on a carried balance over 6 months is usually more. Stabilize the carrying-in-full habit before chasing bonuses.
- **Closing the oldest card.** Usually lowers the score — both through utilization (less total available credit) and eventually through length-of-history.
- **Using cash advances.** Fees plus the no-grace-period APR make this nearly always the wrong move.
- **Opening too many cards too fast.** Multiple hard inquiries in a short window dent the score.
- **Missing the Minimum Payment Warning.** The math is printed by federal requirement. A client who has never looked at it often responds to seeing it more than to a counselor's abstract warning.
- **Assuming all cards give the same fraud protection.** Federal law sets a floor; many issuers exceed it. But recovering fraudulent charges is not instant; disputed charges may take days or weeks. Use credit (not debit) for online purchases and unfamiliar merchants.
