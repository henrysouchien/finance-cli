# Rent vs Buy Decision

Renting versus buying is a housing-structure decision, not a morality test. Renting buys shelter, flexibility, and maintenance transfer. Buying buys shelter plus possible equity, autonomy, and long-term payment stability, while adding transaction costs, maintenance exposure, and foreclosure risk.

This topic supports the decision immediately before home-affordability work. If the user is not clearly committed to buying, the coach should run this comparison before treating a mortgage as the default next step.

## Key Concepts

**Tenure anchors the decision.** AFCPE uses a 5-7 year breakeven heuristic for ownership's high upfront costs to have time to amortize. The exact breakeven varies by market, appreciation, rent growth, tax treatment, interest rate, and selling costs.

**Renting fits some healthy situations.** Renting may fit short expected tenure, unstable income, work mobility, thin down-payment savings, high local prices relative to rent, low appetite for maintenance exposure, or a below-market existing rental.

**Buying fits when the readiness conditions line up.** Buying is stronger when the user expects to stay long enough, has stable income, has down payment plus closing costs plus reserves, has manageable DTI, has good credit, and values stability or autonomy.

**The comparison must use full costs.** Buying cost is not principal-and-interest alone. It is PITI, HOA when applicable, mortgage insurance when applicable, maintenance reserve, and transaction costs. Renting cost should include renter's insurance and expected rent increases.

**The option set is wider than apartment vs single-family home.** Condos, co-ops, manufactured homes, rented houses, duplex units, small homes, ADUs, and roommate strategies can change the economics and lifestyle fit.

## Practical Application

For an AI coach:

- Ask expected tenure first.
- Compare full monthly cost of renting with full monthly cost of ownership.
- Ask whether flexibility, schools, commute, family, autonomy, accessibility, or space is the main driver.
- Check buying readiness with `general_principles.home-affordability`.
- Surface when high-interest debt, thin reserves, or credit-report issues make waiting healthier.
- Route local program and counseling questions to `referrals.hud-approved-housing-counselor`.

## Hand-Off Discipline

The coach may frame tradeoffs, run cost comparisons, and help the user identify the next evidence needed. The coach must hand off when the decision requires specialist judgment.

- Do not predict local appreciation or rent growth as a certainty.
- Do not recommend a specific property, neighborhood, lender, agent, or housing investment.
- Do not treat mortgage-interest or property-tax deductibility as guaranteed tax benefit; route tax-specific questions to tax resources or a preparer.
- Do not interpret lease, purchase, co-op, condo, or manufactured-home legal terms; route legal questions to an attorney or housing counselor.
- Do not decide fair-housing or ECOA claims; help the user preserve facts and route to HUD/CFPB or qualified legal help.

## Common Pitfalls

- Treating renting as "throwing money away" without counting mortgage interest, taxes, insurance, maintenance, and transaction costs.
- Treating homeownership as always financially superior.
- Buying with a short expected tenure.
- Ignoring a below-market rental.
- Assuming mortgage interest will create a tax benefit.
- Underestimating condo/co-op fees and special assessments.
- Buying a manufactured home on rented land without considering lot-rent and mobility risk.
- Stretching to buy before emergency reserves and high-interest debt are under control.
