# Government Financial Assistance Programs

The U.S. safety-net is the income-side complement to the expense-side cuts a coach is usually asking a client to consider. When a household's cash flow has gone persistently negative and there is no remaining expense to compress, the question shifts to whether the household has tapped the federal/state programs that exist precisely for these situations. This topic is the coach's reference for which programs cover which needs and what triggers warrant surfacing them — not for eligibility determination, threshold quotation, or application work.

This topic is **`scope: framing_only`**. The coach educates the client on what exists, frames the trigger conditions for considering it, and routes the client to the correct administering agency or community-services navigator (typically 211 — see `referrals.211`) for the actual eligibility determination and application. The coach does **not** quote income thresholds (FPL-indexed, state-adjusted, frequently revised), determine eligibility, or do application paperwork. See the Hand-Off Discipline section.

## Shared Mechanics Across Programs

Several patterns repeat across the program set and are worth naming once rather than restating per program.

- **Federal funding, state administration.** Most safety-net programs use federal money but are delivered through state or local agencies. Program names, eligibility thresholds within federal floors, asset rules, benefit amounts, and application channels often vary materially by state. The first verification step on any specific case is the client's *state* — not the federal program description.
- **Cross-program coordination is state-specific.** Some states use combined application channels, express-lane eligibility, or "no wrong door" intake that screens for adjacent programs from a single application; others don't. Medicaid and TANF were decoupled in the 1996 welfare-reform era — there is no automatic federal linkage between the two, though individual states may operate combined-application infrastructure or extend categorical eligibility in either direction. The practical answer is state-by-state: don't assume cross-program screening, but do ask whether the state's intake covers multiple programs.
- **Work and time requirements.** Some programs — TANF in particular — carry work-participation requirements and lifetime benefit-time limits. A coaching plan that assumes assistance income continues indefinitely past those limits is building a future crisis in.
- **Stigma is part of the work.** Many clients eligible for assistance never apply because they don't know they qualify, don't believe they do, or feel shame about asking for help. The coach's framing job is to normalize: these programs exist because income disruption happens to everyone, and using them is a financially sound move, not a character verdict.

## Programs

Each entry below is short by design. The body content stays at the level a coach needs to surface awareness and route the client; specific eligibility math and application details belong to the administering program.

### SNAP — Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program

Covers food purchases. Funded under the Food and Nutrition Act (7 U.S.C. § 2011 et seq.), administered nationally by USDA Food and Nutrition Service, delivered through state agencies. Benefits are loaded onto an **Electronic Benefits Transfer (EBT) card** — a debit-style card that works at most grocery stores, many farmers' markets, and a growing set of online retailers for eligible foods. Eligibility runs on household income, assets, and size, with federal floors and state-specific layering. Summer EBT (sometimes branded SUN Bucks at the state level) extends additional food benefits to children during months when school meals aren't available.

### WIC — Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children

Covers nutrition assistance for pregnant women, new mothers, and children up to age five who meet income and nutritional-risk criteria (42 U.S.C. § 1786). USDA-administered. Differs from SNAP structurally: WIC provides specific food items (formula, dairy, produce, grains) plus nutrition counseling and health referrals, rather than a general food benefit. Demographically targeted; does not serve adults outside the qualifying categories.

### TANF — Temporary Assistance for Needy Families

Covers cash assistance for very low-income families with dependent children (42 U.S.C. § 601 et seq.). Federally funded as a block grant; significant program-design discretion at the state level. Program names, eligibility specifics, and benefit amounts vary across states within federal statutory parameters. Carries work-participation requirements and time limits — both as lifetime-cumulative and per-spell caps depending on the state. Cross-program effects vary by state but two relatively durable patterns hold: children in TANF households are typically directly certified for free school meals (USDA direct-certification pathway), and TANF intake at the local human-services office often runs alongside SNAP intake in the same office. Medicaid eligibility is NOT automatic from TANF receipt — Medicaid and TANF were decoupled in 1996; states may operate combined applications or extend categorical Medicaid eligibility to TANF recipients, but that's a state-specific design choice rather than a federal rule. Application generally goes through the local human-services office.

### Medicaid

Covers healthcare for individuals and families with limited income and resources, under Title XIX of the Social Security Act (42 U.S.C. § 1396 et seq.). Joint federal-state program. Federal eligibility floors plus state-administered expansion choices: states that adopted ACA Medicaid expansion cover a meaningfully wider set of low-income adults than non-expansion states do. Coverage typically has little or no recipient cost and spans a comprehensive service range. Unlike ACA marketplace plans, Medicaid and CHIP have no open-enrollment window — applications are accepted year-round, with eligibility taking effect once the state determines it. Specialized state Medicaid programs may target specific populations (e.g., children, pregnant women, disability-qualifying conditions).

### Section 8 — Housing Choice Voucher Program

Covers rental housing in the private market (42 U.S.C. § 1437f). HUD-administered nationally; delivered through local Public Housing Agencies (PHAs). The structure: the program subsidizes private-market rent up to a locally-determined payment standard, with the household contributing a portion based on adjusted income; the voucher covers the gap. Eligible housing types include houses, apartments, townhouses, and mobile homes that meet program health-and-safety standards. Eligibility runs on household income, with priority groups often including elderly applicants, applicants with disabilities, and families with young children.

**Operational reality worth naming:** waiting lists at most PHAs are long; in many high-cost metropolitan areas, lists are closed for extended periods. A coach should surface Section 8 as an option but not present it as a near-term solution unless the local PHA's list status supports that framing.

### LIHEAP — Low Income Home Energy Assistance Program

Covers home energy costs — heating, cooling, emergency utility-shutoff prevention, and energy-related home repairs/weatherization — under the Low Income Home Energy Assistance Act (42 U.S.C. § 8621 et seq.). Federal block grants delivered through state and local agencies. Covers multiple heat sources (electricity, gas, oil). Both homeowners and renters can qualify. Assistance amounts vary by income, household size, and actual energy costs. Often seasonal in timing: heating help in winter, cooling help in summer, with limited emergency funds typically available year-round.

## Practical Application

Coaches surface programs based on observable trigger conditions, not by walking every program in every conversation. Common triggers:

- **Income shock event** (job loss, hours reduction, unpaid leave, an acute medical event) → consider SNAP, TANF where children are part of the household, Medicaid for healthcare bridging. The `general_principles.cash-flow-statement` linkage is direct here — sudden inflow drop without rapid replacement is the canonical trigger condition.
- **Persistent deficit with expense cuts exhausted** (the cash-flow picture has been negative across multiple months and discretionary cuts no longer move the line) → SNAP for food cost, LIHEAP for utility cost, Medicaid for healthcare cost, Section 8 where the housing cost ratio has become unsustainable. The forward spending plan needs to account for benefit-eligibility limits and time caps — see `general_principles.spending-plan` for how assistance income shows up in plan-vs-actual.
- **New child or pregnancy in a low-income household** → consider WIC for nutrition support, Medicaid for pregnancy and postpartum coverage, and TANF screening if cash assistance fits the situation.
- **Utility-shutoff notice or unpaid heating bill at threshold** → LIHEAP is the immediate route; also check for state-level utility-assistance programs that frequently operate in parallel.
- **Crisis intersecting a banking-infrastructure gap** — most programs default to electronic delivery, and clients without a bank account hit friction in both directions (receiving benefits, then converting them out for spending). Fee-bearing prepaid products often fill the gap by default, eroding benefit value. Surface bank-account alternatives in parallel with assistance applications — see `general_principles.banking-basics`.

For an AI coach, program awareness should fire on signals already present in the client's data: sustained cash-flow deficit, recurring utility late-payment fees, client-reported income drop, household-composition change. The framing matters — not a lecture, not a presumption of eligibility, just "have you looked at these?" in a coaching tone with the authoritative link or 211 route attached. The coach should never claim to know whether the client qualifies for any specific program. Eligibility is FPL-indexed, state-specific, and revised on schedules faster than any coach's knowledge base can reliably track.

## Hand-Off Discipline

This topic is `framing_only`. The coach surfaces program awareness, frames trigger conditions, and routes. The coach does **not**:

- **Quote eligibility thresholds.** Income limits, asset limits, and household-size adjustments are FPL-indexed and state-adjusted, with annual or more-frequent revision cycles. A coach quoting a specific threshold is reliably wrong by the time the client applies. Route to the program's authoritative source.
- **Determine eligibility for the client.** Eligibility decisions are the administering program's authority — the state SNAP office, the state Medicaid office, the local PHA, the state LIHEAP administrator. The coach surfaces possibility; the program confirms or denies.
- **Compute benefit amounts.** Benefit calculations interact with household composition, gross-versus-net income, allowable deductions, and state-specific formulas. Out of scope.
- **Assist with documentation gathering or application completion.** Some 211 affiliates and local community-action agencies do offer application-assistance help; that is their role, not the coach's. Route to `referrals.211` for navigator services that can identify which programs the client may want to pursue and what kind of application help is available locally.
- **Handle denial appeals or eligibility disputes.** When a client is denied benefits they appear to qualify for, the route is legal aid (LSC-funded legal services or state-specific legal-aid organizations) or an `attorney` who handles public-benefits disputes. The coach surfaces the appeal-routing option; the legal specialist handles the appeal.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Assuming the client already knows about the programs.** Many eligible clients never apply because they didn't know, didn't believe they qualified, or felt stigma. Surface programs explicitly; don't assume awareness.
- **Quoting eligibility numbers.** Federal floors interact with state rules and household composition in ways that vary widely. A coach who names a specific income threshold during a conversation is going to be wrong often enough that the discipline is to point to the official source instead.
- **Treating programs as interchangeable.** Each program has its own eligibility structure, application channel, and benefit shape. SNAP is not TANF is not Medicaid is not Section 8. Generic "go apply for benefits" guidance misses what the client actually needs.
- **Ignoring TANF time limits.** TANF carries lifetime and per-spell limits that vary by state. A coaching plan that assumes TANF income beyond the limit is building in a downstream cliff.
- **Treating one denial as a verdict on the full assistance picture.** A client whose TANF cash-assistance application is denied may still be eligible for SNAP, Medicaid, LIHEAP, or housing programs — each has its own eligibility structure, and ineligibility for one does not propagate to others. Keep the conversation open after a denial; route the client to surface the other relevant program eligibility independently.
- **Treating the federal list as exhaustive.** Many states run supplemental programs (state rental assistance, state utility funds, child-care subsidies, state-administered emergency grants) that don't appear on any federal program list. Local navigators (211 affiliates, community-action agencies) know what those are. Route there rather than relying on federal-only knowledge.
