# Liquidity Ratio

The liquidity ratio measures how many months a household's liquid assets could cover its monthly expenses if income stopped entirely. It is one of two foundational personal financial ratios (the sibling is debt-to-income — see `general_principles.personal-financial-ratios.debt-to-income`). For most counseling sessions, a low liquidity ratio is the single most common problem surfaced — building liquid savings is the typical first concrete coaching goal.

This topic is the liquidity half of the personal-financial-ratios wiki article; DTI is distilled separately because the two ratios trigger different intervention patterns (liquidity → emergency-fund interventions; DTI → debt-planning interventions) per the wiki-to-topic 1:N cardinality rule.

## Key Concepts

**Formula.**

```
Liquidity Ratio = Liquid Assets / Monthly Expenses
```

The ratio produces a unit-less number that reads as "months of expenses covered." A value of 3 means liquid reserves cover three months of typical spending if income stopped.

**What counts as liquid assets.** Assets readily convertible to cash without significant transaction cost or time:

- Checking and savings accounts
- Money market accounts and money market mutual funds
- Short-term certificates of deposit (CDs)
- Cash held outside the banking system

**What does NOT count.** Assets that take time, transaction cost, or tax penalty to convert:

- Retirement accounts (401(k), IRA, pension) — early-withdrawal penalty + tax
- Real estate (primary residence, investment property) — illiquid, high transaction cost
- Vehicles, collectibles, business equity
- Stocks held in taxable brokerage accounts (technically liquid, but volatility makes them unreliable as emergency reserves)

These assets count on the net worth statement; they do not count for liquidity-runway purposes.

**Monthly expenses denominator.** Use the actual monthly expense total from the cash flow statement, not an idealized post-cut figure. The ratio measures real-world runway, so it has to use real-world spending.

## Recommended Range

**3 to 6 months** per AFCPE financial-planning guidance. The exact target within the band depends on circumstances:

- **Closer to 3 months** — dual-earner households, stable employment, low fixed costs.
- **Closer to 6 months** — single-earner households, variable-income workers (commission, freelance, seasonal), heavy fixed costs, or industries with longer typical job-search durations.
- **Higher than 6 months** — appropriate during anticipated income disruption (planned career transition, pre-retirement) or when the household has dependents requiring extra cushion.

## Worked Example

A two-earner family with liquid assets of $6,800 (checking $2,800 + savings $4,000) and total monthly expenses of $6,100:

```
Liquidity Ratio = $6,800 / $6,100 ≈ 1.1 months
```

This is well below the 3–6 month recommendation. The family would run out of liquid cash in just over a month if income stopped. The coaching priority is building the liquid reserve before focusing on other financial goals — even before accelerated debt paydown in most cases. A shock during heavy debt paydown typically resurfaces as new credit-card debt, undoing the paydown progress.

## Interpretation

- **Low liquidity is the most common counseling problem.** Building liquid savings is the typical first concrete goal in many engagements. Other financial work (debt paydown, retirement contributions, investment allocation) often waits until liquidity is at least at the lower end of the recommended range.
- **Liquidity is not net worth.** A household with significant retirement assets and minimal checking balances can have strong net worth and dangerous liquidity. Both signals matter; neither is sufficient on its own.
- **Two headwinds at once.** A month of overspending both shrinks the numerator (liquid balances drop) and can raise the denominator (if it raises baseline expenses). The ratio updates whenever either side moves.
- **Liquidity protects debt-paydown progress.** Counseling practice typically prioritizes a starter emergency fund (often $1,000 to one month of expenses) BEFORE aggressive debt paydown, then continues building liquidity in parallel with debt work.

## Practical Application

For an AI coach, liquidity is the ratio most likely to surface a specific action. Once liquid balances and monthly expenses are computable from account data and transaction history, the ratio falls out automatically. Display it with context — not "Liquidity ratio: 0.8 months" in isolation, but "Liquidity ratio: 0.8 months — well below the 3-month minimum most planners recommend; this is the single biggest risk in your current setup, and building a starter emergency fund is the highest-leverage move you can make right now."

The conversation that follows is concrete: how much per month can the household reasonably contribute to a dedicated savings account? What is the realistic time-to-3-months target? Is automation (pay-yourself-first transfer on payday) a fit?

Watch for the false-comfort case: a household with high net worth concentrated in retirement and home equity but thin liquid balances. The ratios surface what the totals hide.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Counting retirement or real estate as liquid.** They are assets but not liquid. Including them produces a meaningless emergency-runway number.
- **Counting taxable brokerage stocks as liquid.** Technically convertible to cash within days, but market volatility makes them unreliable as a true emergency reserve. Some practitioners include them with a haircut; for counseling purposes, the conservative practice is to exclude.
- **Using post-cut expense estimates.** "If we cut everything we could, we'd only spend $4,000 a month" is a hypothetical. The ratio uses actual recent spending, not an emergency-mode budget.
- **Treating the benchmark as a goal.** "Hit 6 months and stop" misses the point. Liquidity needs are circumstantial; the right target updates as life updates (new kid, single-earner transition, near-retirement).
- **Computing once.** Liquidity is a snapshot. Recompute when balances or spending change — monthly for working households, more often during volatile periods.
