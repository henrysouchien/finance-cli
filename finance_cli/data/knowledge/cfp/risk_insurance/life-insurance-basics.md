# Life Insurance Basics

Life insurance is a contract that may pay a death benefit when the insured dies
and the policy conditions are met. In planning, the first question is not "what
should I buy?" It is "who would be financially harmed by this person's death,
what facts are missing, and what professional review is needed?"

CashNerd can explain vocabulary and preserve beneficiary-review prompts. It
does not recommend a policy, insurer, coverage amount, rider, premium,
replacement, surrender, loan, or beneficiary choice.

## Key Concepts

**Death benefit.** The amount payable under the policy if the claim qualifies.
Whether a claim qualifies depends on policy terms and facts.

**Beneficiary.** The person or entity named to receive the death benefit.
Beneficiary designations can interact with estate planning and should be
reviewed after life events.

**Term insurance.** Coverage intended for a stated period. It is often discussed
for temporary needs such as income replacement during child-rearing years or a
mortgage period, but the suitability of any term is a policy and planning
decision.

**Permanent insurance.** Coverage designed for longer duration. Some policies
include cash value or investment-like features. These features add complexity,
cost, tax, and suitability questions that route to qualified review.

**Group versus individual coverage.** Employer or association coverage may be
convenient but may not be portable, sufficient, or permanent. The details come
from benefits documents.

**Policy locator.** When a beneficiary suspects a policy exists but cannot find
it, NAIC offers a life insurance policy locator. CashNerd may point the user to
public resources without promising a result.

## Planning Use

Gather the user's facts at a checklist level:

- dependents or people financially relying on the insured;
- debts, mortgage, childcare, education, business, or caregiving obligations;
- existing group or individual life insurance;
- policy owner, insured person, and beneficiary status if known;
- last beneficiary review date;
- major life events since the last review;
- permanent-policy cash value, loans, surrender charges, or tax issues if the
  user volunteers them;
- whether an estate attorney or fiduciary planner is already involved.

When facts are missing, keep them missing. Do not calculate or suggest a
coverage amount in this v0.1 topic.

## Estate And Beneficiary Crossover

Life insurance often sits outside the will through beneficiary designation. That
can be useful, but it also means stale beneficiary forms can create results the
user no longer expects. Route legal effect, minor-beneficiary issues, trust
ownership, estate tax, creditor, divorce, and business-succession questions to
an attorney or qualified professional.

## Hand-Off Discipline

The coach may:

- explain term, permanent, beneficiary, owner, insured, premium, and death
  benefit vocabulary;
- prompt the user to locate policies and benefits summaries;
- flag stale-beneficiary review after life events;
- prepare questions for an insurance professional, fiduciary planner, HR, or an
  estate attorney.

The coach must route out for:

- purchase, replacement, surrender, lapse, loan, or withdrawal decisions;
- recommending a face amount, product type, rider, insurer, or premium tradeoff;
- suitability analysis for permanent insurance or annuities;
- tax treatment of policy loans, withdrawals, or employer coverage;
- beneficiary legal effect, trust ownership, divorce, minor beneficiary, or
  estate-tax planning.

## Common Pitfalls

- Buying coverage before clarifying the obligation being protected.
- Assuming workplace coverage follows the user after a job change.
- Forgetting to review beneficiaries after marriage, divorce, birth, death, or
  estrangement.
- Treating cash value as simple savings.
- Replacing or surrendering a policy without qualified review.
