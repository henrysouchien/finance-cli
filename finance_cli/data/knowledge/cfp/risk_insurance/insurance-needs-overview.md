# Insurance Needs Overview

Insurance planning starts with risk transfer, not with a product pitch. The
household identifies events that could create a large or destabilizing cost,
then decides which risks to retain through cash reserves and which risks may
need an insurance policy or professional review.

CashNerd can help the user build an inventory, spot missing facts, and prepare
questions. It does not recommend a specific insurer, policy, rider, coverage
amount, premium, cancellation, replacement, or underwriting action.

## Key Concepts

**Risk exposure.** A risk exposure is a plausible event that can damage the
household balance sheet or cash flow. Examples include medical care, disability,
death of an income earner, property loss, liability claims, long-term care,
business interruption, or natural disaster.

**Risk retention.** Some risks are intentionally retained. A deductible,
self-insured small repair, or emergency reserve is a way to absorb a bounded
cost without transferring it to an insurer.

**Risk transfer.** Insurance transfers some financial consequences to an
insurer under a contract. The policy terms, exclusions, deductibles, limits, and
claims process control the actual protection; the coach should not infer those
details without documents and professional review.

**Planning dependency.** Risk gaps can change the next-best financial move. A
user with thin cash reserves, unknown health out-of-pocket exposure, no
disability-income context, or unreviewed dependents may need to pause aggressive
investing, homebuying, or debt-prepayment plans until the risk picture is
clearer.

**State-based insurance system.** Insurance oversight and consumer assistance
often route through state insurance departments. Users with complaints, agent
licensing questions, or state-specific coverage questions should use the
appropriate state department or licensed professional.

## Readiness Inventory

For `coach_risk_insurance_readiness`, gather high-level facts first:

- household members, dependents, and caregiving obligations;
- employment context and employer benefits;
- homeownership, renting, vehicle ownership, business ownership, or gig work;
- emergency reserve months and essential monthly expenses;
- known health, disability, life, property, liability, and long-term-care
  coverage;
- deductible, out-of-pocket maximum, beneficiary, policy-review, and renewal
  dates when the user already has them;
- open claims, complaints, denials, lapses, or cancellation notices;
- professional relationships already in place.

Unknown facts should remain data gaps. Do not fill them with generic coverage
amounts or sales heuristics.

## Planning Use

Use this topic to classify the risk conversation:

- **education_only:** the user wants vocabulary or a map of what to review;
- **data_needed:** the user cannot identify current coverage, deductibles,
  beneficiaries, or employer benefits;
- **review_recommended:** the user has facts but a licensed professional should
  review suitability or product details;
- **risk_gap:** a missing or weak protection could materially affect the next
  financial decision;
- **ready:** the user has a clear enough risk inventory for the adjacent
  planning workflow;
- **refer:** a policy, claim, legal, licensing, complaint, or regulated advice
  question requires handoff.

The output should explain why the classification matters for the next planning
step.

## Hand-Off Discipline

The coach may:

- explain broad insurance categories and vocabulary;
- help the user list current policies and missing facts;
- relate deductibles and reserves to cash-flow planning;
- prepare questions for HR, benefits teams, insurers, agents, or state
  departments;
- preserve a referral rationale in the artifact.

The coach must route out for:

- policy selection, replacement, cancellation, or coverage-amount decisions;
- insurer or agent recommendations;
- claim disputes, denial appeals, rescission, fraud accusations, or complaint
  strategy;
- underwriting eligibility or premium predictions;
- legal interpretation of policy language;
- Medicare, Medigap, annuity, long-term-care, business-insurance, or estate-tax
  planning where specialist rules control the decision.

## Common Pitfalls

- Optimizing investments while ignoring catastrophic downside risk.
- Treating the lowest premium as the best policy.
- Forgetting that deductibles and exclusions can leave major retained risk.
- Assuming employer benefits are permanent or portable.
- Letting beneficiary designations go stale after life events.
- Waiting until a claim or renewal deadline to understand the policy.
