# Health Insurance Cost Sharing

Health insurance cost sharing is the part of covered care the household may
still pay after buying coverage. For planning, the important distinction is
between the monthly premium and the costs that appear when care is used.

This is a vocabulary topic. CashNerd may help the user understand terms and
capture numbers from plan documents. It does not choose a health plan, decide
eligibility, estimate subsidies, sell coverage, or interpret disputed claims.

## Key Concepts

**Premium.** The recurring amount paid to keep coverage active. Premiums affect
monthly cash flow even if the user has no care that month.

**Deductible.** The amount the user pays for covered services before the plan
begins paying for many services. Some preventive or plan-specific services may
be covered before the deductible, so the actual rule comes from the plan.

**Copayment.** A fixed charge for a covered service, such as a visit or
prescription, when the plan uses that structure.

**Coinsurance.** A percentage share of a covered cost that the user pays after
the plan's rules apply.

**Out-of-pocket maximum.** A plan-year cap on the user's cost sharing for
covered in-network services under the plan's rules. Premiums and noncovered
services are not treated the same way as covered cost sharing.

**Network and formulary.** The providers, facilities, pharmacies, and drugs
covered at preferred terms. Network and formulary gaps can matter as much as
deductible size for a household with known care needs.

## Planning Use

When a user asks whether a financial plan is safe, gather:

- monthly premium;
- deductible;
- out-of-pocket maximum;
- whether individual and family deductibles or caps both apply;
- recurring prescriptions, expected procedures, or known provider needs;
- HSA/FSA/HDHP context when relevant;
- whether the plan is employer, marketplace, Medicare, Medicaid, COBRA,
  student, spouse/partner, or other coverage;
- enrollment or renewal deadline.

For a readiness artifact, do not treat "has health insurance" as complete
context. Record whether the deductible and out-of-pocket maximum are known,
whether reserves can absorb them, and whether the user needs benefits-team or
licensed help before making related decisions.

## Cash-Flow Framing

Two users with the same premium can have different risk exposure:

- one has low recurring care, a manageable deductible, and reserves above the
  out-of-pocket maximum;
- another has the same premium but high prescription needs, an unknown network,
  and cash reserves below the likely first-dollar cost of care.

The coach should make that difference visible without recommending a plan.

## Hand-Off Discipline

The coach may:

- define terms;
- ask the user to locate values on a summary of benefits and coverage;
- compare known cost-sharing numbers to current emergency reserves;
- prepare enrollment questions for HR, the marketplace, Medicare/SHIP, Medicaid,
  or an insurance professional.

The coach must route out for:

- selecting a plan, network, insurer, metal tier, Medicare plan, Medigap policy,
  or drug formulary;
- subsidy, tax-credit, Medicaid, or Medicare eligibility determinations;
- appeals, claim denials, medical necessity disputes, or billing disputes;
- medical advice about whether or when to seek care.

## Common Pitfalls

- Comparing plans only by premium.
- Ignoring the out-of-pocket maximum when setting reserve targets.
- Assuming a provider or drug is in network because it was covered last year.
- Forgetting that family plans may have both individual and family cost-sharing
  rules.
- Treating a deductible as the total worst-case cost.
