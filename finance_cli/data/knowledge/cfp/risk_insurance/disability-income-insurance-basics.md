# Disability Income Insurance Basics

Disability income insurance is protection against loss of earned income when an
illness or injury prevents work under the policy's terms. It matters in
financial planning because the user's income is often the asset funding housing,
debt payments, savings, benefits, and family support.

This topic gives vocabulary and readiness checks. CashNerd does not recommend a
specific policy, benefit amount, waiting period, rider, insurer, or replacement
transaction.

## Key Concepts

**Income replacement.** Disability coverage generally replaces a portion of
income, not every dollar of household spending. A readiness conversation should
compare known benefits to essential expenses and reserves.

**Short-term versus long-term.** Short-term disability coverage is designed for
shorter absences. Long-term disability coverage is designed for longer periods
and may begin after an elimination period.

**Elimination period.** The waiting period before benefits begin. A longer
waiting period increases the need for liquid reserves.

**Benefit period.** The maximum time benefits may be paid if the policy's
definition of disability and claim requirements are met.

**Definition of disability.** Policies can define disability differently. The
difference between own-occupation, any-occupation, partial, residual, and other
definitions is a policy-review issue, not something the coach should infer.

**Tax context.** Whether benefits are taxable can depend on how premiums are
paid and the benefit arrangement. That belongs in data gathering and CPA or
benefits-team review when material.

## Planning Use

For `coach_risk_insurance_readiness`, ask for facts the user can identify:

- employer short-term and long-term disability availability;
- individual policy existence;
- monthly benefit or replacement percentage if known;
- elimination period and benefit period if known;
- whether self-employment changes the coverage picture;
- essential monthly expenses and emergency reserve months;
- dependents or obligations that rely on the user's income;
- open enrollment, underwriting, or review deadlines.

If the user cannot identify these facts, the status should be `data_needed` or
`review_recommended`, not a guessed coverage target.

## Cross-Skill Implications

Thin disability-income context can affect:

- investing readiness, because long-horizon contributions may be fragile when
  earned income protection is unknown;
- homebuying readiness, because mortgage obligations can outlive an income
  interruption;
- debt payoff, because aggressive prepayment may reduce the cash buffer needed
  to cover an elimination period;
- retirement contribution readiness, because the user's ability to continue
  payroll contributions depends on income continuity.

The artifact should describe the planning implication without prescribing a
policy.

## Hand-Off Discipline

The coach may:

- explain terms;
- help the user locate benefits summaries or policy declarations;
- compare known waiting periods to liquid reserves;
- draft questions for HR, a benefits administrator, an insurance professional,
  or a fiduciary planner.

The coach must route out for:

- choosing individual versus employer coverage;
- recommending a benefit amount, rider, waiting period, or policy type;
- evaluating replacement or cancellation of an existing policy;
- underwriting, claim, denial, or appeal strategy;
- tax treatment of benefits when it affects a filing or planning decision.

## Common Pitfalls

- Assuming employer coverage exists without checking benefits documents.
- Treating emergency savings and disability coverage as interchangeable.
- Ignoring self-employment or variable-income exposure.
- Forgetting that a benefit may be capped below the user's actual income.
- Waiting until after a health event to investigate coverage.
