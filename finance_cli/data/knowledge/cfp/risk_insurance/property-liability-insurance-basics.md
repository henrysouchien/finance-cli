# Property And Liability Insurance Basics

Property and liability insurance protects against damage to property and legal
responsibility for certain injuries or losses, subject to policy terms. For
financial planning, the key task is to know what property, vehicles, homes,
rentals, and liability exposures exist before relying on the balance sheet.

This is a vocabulary topic. CashNerd may help the user inventory facts and
prepare questions, but it does not recommend a policy, insurer, limit,
deductible, endorsement, replacement, cancellation, or claim strategy.

## Key Concepts

**Property coverage.** Coverage for a home, belongings, vehicle, or other
property depends on the policy and named risks. The user should not assume all
losses are covered.

**Liability coverage.** Liability coverage may respond when the insured is
legally responsible for injury or property damage under the policy's terms. The
coach should not assess legal fault or policy coverage.

**Deductible.** The amount the user pays out of pocket for a covered claim
before insurance pays under the policy. Higher deductibles can lower premiums
but require stronger cash reserves.

**Replacement cost versus actual cash value.** Some policies value property
based on replacement cost while others account for depreciation. The difference
can change the household's retained risk.

**Excluded or separate risks.** Flood, earthquake, wildfire, windstorm, sewer
backup, business use, rideshare, short-term rental, jewelry, collectibles, and
home-office exposures may require separate coverage, endorsements, or review.

**Lender-required coverage.** A mortgage lender may require homeowners insurance
while a loan is outstanding, but lender requirements protect the collateral and
do not prove the user's coverage is sufficient for household planning.

## Planning Use

For a risk-readiness artifact, gather:

- whether the user owns, rents, or lives with family;
- mortgage, lease, HOA, condo, co-op, or landlord requirements if known;
- homeowners, renters, condo, auto, umbrella, flood, or other policies;
- deductible amounts and renewal dates if known;
- lender, landlord, or state-required minimums if relevant;
- business, gig-work, rideshare, short-term-rental, or home-office uses;
- recent claims, nonrenewal, cancellation, coverage disputes, or complaint
  concerns;
- emergency reserve available for deductibles and uncovered repairs.

Missing policy details should produce a data gap or professional-review next
action, not a guessed coverage recommendation.

## Planning Implications

Property and liability gaps can affect:

- homebuying readiness, because mortgage approval and actual household risk are
  different;
- emergency fund targets, because deductibles and excluded losses create retained
  risk;
- debt payoff and investing, because aggressive cash deployment can leave the
  user unable to cover deductibles or repairs;
- business or self-employment planning, because personal policies may not cover
  business exposures.

## Hand-Off Discipline

The coach may:

- explain terms such as deductible, premium, liability, replacement cost, actual
  cash value, exclusion, endorsement, and umbrella;
- help the user list policies and renewal dates;
- compare known deductibles to available cash reserves;
- prepare questions for an insurance professional, landlord, lender, HOA, or
  state insurance department.

The coach must route out for:

- selecting limits, deductibles, endorsements, insurers, or umbrella coverage;
- deciding whether to cancel, replace, or bundle policies;
- claim coverage, denial, liability, fault, or legal strategy;
- interpreting policy wording, lease requirements, HOA documents, or lender
  notices;
- state-specific insurance law or complaint strategy.

## Common Pitfalls

- Assuming homeowners insurance covers flood or earthquake.
- Choosing a high deductible without keeping cash to pay it.
- Letting a lender's minimum become the household's only coverage review.
- Ignoring business, rideshare, rental, or home-office uses.
- Failing to update coverage after renovations, major purchases, or household
  changes.
- Treating a claim dispute as a budgeting problem instead of a professional or
  regulatory question.
