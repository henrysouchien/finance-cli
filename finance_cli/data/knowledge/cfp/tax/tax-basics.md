# Tax Basics

Tax basics are a cash-flow topic before they are a filing-season topic. For most working households, taxes operate underneath every paycheck: gross pay becomes net pay after federal and state withholding, Social Security, Medicare, benefits, and other deductions. The annual return reconciles that year of withholding against the final tax calculation.

This topic is **`scope: framing_only`**. The coach can explain mechanics, identify data the user should gather, and point to IRS or preparer resources. The coach does not prepare returns, declare credit eligibility, or give tax-specific legal advice. Current-year dollar figures belong in year-specific topics or live official-source checks, not here.

## Core Mechanics

- **Income tax is reconciled annually.** Federal and often state income tax is withheld during the year, then reconciled on the annual return.
- **FICA is separate from income tax.** Social Security and Medicare payroll taxes are withheld from wages. Self-employed taxpayers pay the employee and employer sides through self-employment tax mechanics.
- **Deductions reduce taxable income.** The taxpayer generally uses either the standard deduction or itemized deductions. Itemizing only matters when eligible itemized expenses exceed the standard deduction and records support the claim.
- **Credits reduce tax directly.** A credit is usually more valuable than a deduction of the same face amount. Refundable credits can generate or increase a refund even when income-tax liability is low.
- **Withholding controls refund or balance due.** A recurring large refund usually means the user over-withheld during the year; a recurring bill usually means under-withholding or missing estimated payments.

## Coaching Use

Use this topic when:

- The user asks why their take-home pay is much lower than gross pay.
- A goal is stuck but the user receives a recurring large refund that could be converted into monthly cash flow.
- The user had a filing-time bill and needs a safer withholding or estimated-payment rhythm.
- The user reports a life event that changes withholding assumptions: marriage, divorce, child, second job, job loss, self-employment income, home purchase, retirement, or dependent changes.
- The user may benefit from awareness of EITC, Child Tax Credit, education credits, or Saver's Credit, while eligibility remains for IRS tools or a preparer to determine.

## Withholding Discipline

The coaching target is usually a small refund or small balance due, not a maximized refund. However, strategy fit matters. Some users deliberately use refunds as forced savings; reducing withholding without an alternative savings rail can make them worse off. The coach should model, ask, and then help route the extra pay-period cash into a goal or spending plan only if the user wants that change.

When a user wants to change withholding:

1. Use the IRS Tax Withholding Estimator or a qualified preparer to model the current year.
2. Gather recent pay statements, expected income, filing status, dependents, other income, deductions, and prior refund or balance-due pattern.
3. Submit an updated W-4 to payroll or HR only after the user understands the tradeoff.
4. Recheck after one or two pay cycles and again after life events.

## Boundaries

The coach must avoid:

- Quoting current-year brackets, standard deduction amounts, credit thresholds, or wage bases without an authoritative current-year source.
- Declaring that the user qualifies for a credit or filing status.
- Choosing itemized versus standard deduction as tax advice; the coach can explain the threshold concept and point to records or preparer review.
- Preparing or reviewing a return line by line.
- Treating federal-only logic as complete when state or local tax may matter.
