# Investment Readiness

Investment readiness answers a planning question before any investment account is funded: does moving cash toward long-term investing fit the user's current cash flow, reserves, debt, goals, and time horizon?

This topic supports readiness framing and cash-flow sequencing. It does not choose securities, funds, model portfolios, asset-allocation targets, brokerages, tax positions, or trades.

## Readiness Statuses

Use these statuses when the user asks whether they are ready to invest.

**education_only.** The user is exploring, anxious, or vocabulary-limited. Explain the map and ask what "invest" means to them before producing a plan.

**data_needed.** Key facts are missing: cash reserves, essential spending, debt rates, goal horizon, retirement-plan match, tax-advantaged room, or the target account type.

**fix_first.** A near-term risk should be handled before funding an investment account. Common triggers are very thin emergency savings, high-interest debt, unstable surplus, or a short-term goal that would force a market sale.

**cash_ready.** The user has plausible surplus after reserves, debt, and near-term obligations, but has not confirmed the account type, contribution room, destination account, or professional boundary.

**account_funding_ready.** The user can reasonably fund a user-owned investment account as a cash-movement action, with no securities or allocation recommendation attached.

**refer.** The user wants security selection, individualized allocation, tax execution, rollover execution, complex compensation planning, or regulated investment advice.

## Readiness Checks

**Emergency reserve.** A dedicated emergency fund protects the plan from common shocks such as repairs, medical bills, or income loss. If the user has little liquid runway, a starter reserve or full emergency-fund plan usually comes before taxable investing.

**High-interest debt.** Investor.gov frames high-interest debt payoff as a strong pre-investing priority because eliminating that interest is a lower-risk use of cash than trying to out-earn it in markets. Use `general_principles.debt-vs-investing-decision-frame` and `advisory_debt_vs_invest` when the rates and balances are known.

**Cash-flow surplus.** Readiness is not "has money in checking today." It is recurring surplus after expected income, essential expenses, debt minimums, required bills, and planned near-term obligations.

**Time horizon and liquidity need.** Money needed in the next few years belongs in a different conversation from money intended for retirement or another long-term goal. Short horizons increase the risk that the user must sell during a bad market period.

**Tax-advantaged sequence.** If an employer match, IRA room, HSA eligibility, or workplace-plan room may be relevant, gather facts and use source-backed helpers. Do not quote tax-year limits from memory.

**Account clarity.** Know whether the target is a taxable brokerage account, IRA, workplace plan, HSA, or an unknown account type. Account type affects tax treatment, contribution limits, withdrawal rules, and support boundaries.

**Advice boundary.** Funding an account is not the same as choosing what to buy. The plan must label any funding action as cash movement only.

## Practical Application

For the investment-readiness skill, the first useful output is usually a one-page readiness plan:

- current status;
- facts used and facts missing;
- reserve, debt, surplus, goal-horizon, and account-type checks;
- possible next actions;
- a funding amount and cadence only when the user is account-funding-ready;
- a clear statement that the plan does not select securities or a portfolio.

The coach should present multiple options before narrowing. Example options include: build cash reserve first, accelerate high-interest debt payoff, capture employer match, gather IRA/workplace-plan data, create a manual account-funding checklist, or prepare a professional handoff.

## Hand-Off Discipline

Refer to a fiduciary adviser, investment adviser, plan provider, CPA, or attorney when the user asks for:

- security, fund, ETF, crypto, bond, option, or brokerage recommendations;
- individualized asset-allocation percentages;
- rebalancing, tax-loss harvesting, rollover, Roth conversion, or asset-location execution;
- self-employed retirement-plan setup;
- legal, tax filing, or securities-law conclusions.

## Common Pitfalls

- Treating "extra cash this month" as readiness without checking recurring surplus.
- Funding taxable investing while employer match is unknown.
- Ignoring high-interest debt because investing feels more exciting.
- Counting a volatile brokerage account as the emergency reserve.
- Turning a risk-tolerance conversation into a target allocation.
- Letting a cash-movement plan imply security selection.
