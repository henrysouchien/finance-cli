# Risk Capacity and Risk Tolerance

Risk language has two different layers.

**Risk capacity** is the user's financial ability to take volatility or loss without breaking the plan. It comes from time horizon, liquidity needs, income stability, debt load, emergency reserve, fixed obligations, dependents, goal deadlines, and whether the user would need to sell during a bad market.

**Risk tolerance** is the user's willingness and emotional ability to live with volatility. It comes from experience, temperament, prior losses, sleep-at-night concerns, and stated preferences.

Both matter. Neither should be converted into a portfolio allocation by this topic.

## Risk Capacity Inputs

The coach can evaluate capacity with factual planning inputs:

- **Time horizon.** Long goals can usually absorb more volatility than short goals because the user has more time before cash is needed.
- **Liquidity need.** Money needed for rent, taxes, debt payments, medical expenses, tuition, or a near-term purchase has lower risk capacity.
- **Emergency reserve.** Thin reserves increase the chance that the user sells investments during a shock.
- **Debt burden.** High-interest or high-payment debt reduces flexibility and may make debt payoff the better next use of cash.
- **Income stability.** Variable, seasonal, commission, freelance, or single-earner income usually lowers capacity.
- **Goal rigidity.** A flexible retirement date is different from a down payment needed in 18 months.

These factors can support a readiness status, a data-needed list, or a professional handoff. They do not produce a user-specific allocation inside CashNerd.

## Risk Tolerance Inputs

Risk tolerance is partly behavioral:

- Has the user invested through downturns before?
- How did they react to prior losses?
- Would a 10%, 20%, or 30% temporary drop cause them to sell?
- Are they trying to avoid anxiety, maximize growth, or balance both?
- Do they understand that all securities carry some degree of risk?

The coach should treat tolerance statements as data, not as a complete answer. A user can be emotionally comfortable with risk but financially unable to take it. A user can have strong capacity but low tolerance, which may require education or a fiduciary handoff rather than pressure.

## Relationship to Asset Allocation

Investor.gov frames asset allocation as a personal decision affected by time horizon and risk tolerance. FINRA's suitability rule lists investment profile factors such as financial situation and needs, tax status, objectives, experience, time horizon, liquidity needs, and risk tolerance.

For CashNerd, that means:

- explain what the factors are;
- gather and summarize them;
- use them to decide whether account funding is premature;
- do not turn them into a target allocation or trade instruction.

## Practical Application

Use risk capacity and tolerance to support statuses:

- **data_needed:** time horizon, reserve, income stability, or debt data is missing.
- **fix_first:** liquidity, debt, or short-horizon constraints dominate.
- **cash_ready:** capacity appears plausible, but account or tax details are missing.
- **account_funding_ready:** funding is plausible as cash movement only.
- **refer:** the user wants allocation, product selection, or trade implementation.

## Common Pitfalls

- Treating a risk questionnaire as a recommendation engine.
- Using "aggressive" or "conservative" labels without factual context.
- Ignoring the user's need to access cash soon.
- Assuming long horizon alone is enough when reserves are thin.
- Telling the user a specific allocation follows from their answers.
