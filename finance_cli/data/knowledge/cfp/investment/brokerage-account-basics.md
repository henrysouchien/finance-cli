# Brokerage Account Basics

A brokerage account is an investment account used to hold cash and buy or sell securities. It is different from a bank savings account, retirement plan, or advisory engagement, even when the same financial company offers several of those services.

This topic is vocabulary and handoff support. It does not recommend a brokerage firm, account provider, securities, funds, allocation, or trade.

## Key Concepts

**Account type matters.** A taxable brokerage account, IRA, workplace plan, HSA, and advisory account can all support investing, but they have different tax treatment, contribution rules, fees, withdrawal rules, and service models.

**Cash in the account is not the same as being invested.** A user may transfer money into a brokerage account and leave it in a cash position until they or an authorized professional decide what to buy.

**The account owner controls the relationship.** Funding plans should use a user-owned destination account. The coach should not create vendor preference, provider ranking, or hidden default brokerage guidance.

**Fees and services differ.** Trading commissions, advisory fees, expense ratios, cash sweep rates, margin terms, account minimums, and support models can vary. The coach can tell the user to review disclosures; it should not choose the provider.

**Statements and confirmations matter.** Investor.gov tells users with brokerage or advisory accounts to read statements promptly and object in writing if something is wrong. The coach can remind users to retain and review account records.

**Professional checks matter.** Investor.gov encourages checking whether an investment professional is licensed and registered. If the user is working with an adviser or broker, route them to official lookup tools rather than relying on titles.

## Funding Checklist

Before a cash-movement-only account-funding plan, confirm:

- account type and ownership;
- whether the destination accepts the funding method;
- source account, amount, cadence, and timing;
- emergency reserve and debt checks;
- tax-year or plan-provider constraints when the account is retirement or HSA related;
- whether the user understands that funding does not choose investments.

## Hand-Off Discipline

Refer or defer when the user asks:

- which brokerage to use;
- whether a broker or adviser is best for them;
- what securities or funds to buy;
- what allocation to use;
- whether to use margin, options, short selling, crypto, private funds, or complex products;
- how to interpret legal, tax, or advisory contract terms.

## Common Pitfalls

- Assuming every brokerage account is taxable.
- Treating a transfer into the account as a trade.
- Ignoring cash sweep, margin, and fee disclosures.
- Choosing a provider because it is familiar to the coach or app.
- Trusting professional titles without checking registration.
- Missing that retirement and HSA accounts have contribution and eligibility rules outside ordinary brokerage vocabulary.
