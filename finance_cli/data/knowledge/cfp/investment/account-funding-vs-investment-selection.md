# Account Funding vs. Investment Selection

Account funding and investment selection are separate decisions.

**Account funding** is a cash-flow action: moving money into a user-owned investment account, retirement account, HSA, or other eligible destination. It answers "how much cash can reasonably move, how often, and from which source?"

**Investment selection** is an implementation decision inside the account: what securities, funds, asset classes, or trades to hold. It answers "what should I buy, sell, hold, rebalance, or allocate to?"

CashNerd can support the first decision when readiness gates pass. It must not pretend the first decision solves the second.

## Allowed Funding Guidance

The coach may help with:

- confirming the user's goal and time horizon;
- checking emergency reserve, debt, cash-flow surplus, and near-term goal conflicts;
- identifying whether the target account is taxable, IRA, workplace plan, HSA, or unknown;
- drafting a cash-movement-only funding plan;
- labeling the destination account without recommending a provider;
- asking the user to review transfer details through the web authorization path when a provider supports draft intents.

Every funding plan should include the label `cash_movement_only` or equivalent wording. It should say that the plan does not choose investments, recommend an allocation, or place trades.

## Prohibited Selection Guidance

Do not recommend:

- a specific stock, ETF, mutual fund, bond, crypto asset, option, annuity, or private investment;
- a specific allocation such as "80/20," "60/40," or "all index funds" for this user;
- market timing, rebalancing instructions, tax-loss harvesting, rollovers, or Roth conversions;
- a specific brokerage, adviser, robo-adviser, or product provider.

The coach may explain concepts generally, but when the user asks what to buy or how to allocate, route to education plus a fiduciary/RIA handoff.

## Why the Distinction Matters

A user can be ready to move $250 per month into a long-term account and still need professional or provider guidance for investment selection. Conversely, a user can understand funds and diversification but still be cash-flow-not-ready because reserves are thin or debt is too expensive.

The skill should decide the cash-flow side first. Selection questions are a boundary trigger, not a reason to abandon readiness planning.

## Practical Application

Good phrasing:

- "You appear account-funding-ready for a cash movement of up to $250 per month, assuming the destination account is yours and ACH-enabled."
- "This does not decide what to buy inside the account."
- "If you want help choosing investments or a target allocation, that belongs with a registered adviser or your plan provider."

Bad phrasing:

- "Move $250/month into a brokerage and buy ETF X."
- "Your risk score means 80% stocks."
- "Use Brokerage Y."
- "Rebalance quarterly into this model portfolio."

## Common Pitfalls

- Treating a brokerage deposit as if it were already invested.
- Calling a cash transfer an "investment recommendation."
- Naming a destination account type without checking tax-year or plan-provider constraints.
- Allowing allocation examples to become user-specific targets.
- Creating a money-movement intent without explicit user review.
