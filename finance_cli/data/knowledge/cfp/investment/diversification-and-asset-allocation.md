# Diversification and Asset Allocation

Diversification and asset allocation are core investment vocabulary, but this topic is education only.

**Asset allocation** means dividing investments among categories such as stocks, bonds, and cash. Investor.gov frames the decision as personal and tied to factors such as time horizon and risk tolerance.

**Diversification** means spreading money across different investments or categories so that one holding, sector, issuer, or asset class does not dominate the outcome. Diversification can reduce some risks, but it does not remove investment risk or guarantee against loss.

## Education Boundary

The coach may explain:

- what asset classes are at a high level;
- why time horizon and risk tolerance matter;
- why concentration risk is different from broad-market risk;
- why diversification can still fail during market-wide declines;
- why rebalancing is a concept users may discuss with an adviser or plan provider.

The coach must not:

- recommend a target allocation for the user;
- name securities, funds, ETFs, bonds, crypto assets, or products to buy;
- state that a model portfolio is suitable for the user;
- give rebalancing instructions;
- imply that funding an account means the account is diversified.

## Practical Application

Use this topic when the user asks:

- "What is diversification?"
- "What does asset allocation mean?"
- "Why does horizon matter?"
- "Does moving money into a brokerage mean it is invested?"

If the user asks "what allocation should I use?" or "which ETF should I buy?", switch to boundary language and handoff:

- explain the general factors that professionals consider;
- summarize the user's known readiness context;
- offer to prepare questions for a registered adviser, plan provider, or fiduciary planner.

## Common Pitfalls

- Treating one broad-sounding fund name as proof of diversification.
- Assuming a cash deposit into a brokerage account is already invested.
- Saying diversification eliminates risk.
- Giving sample percentages that the user may treat as their own allocation.
- Ignoring concentration in employer stock, crypto, a single sector, or one account.
